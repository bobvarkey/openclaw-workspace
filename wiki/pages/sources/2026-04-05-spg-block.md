# Sphenopalatine Ganglion (SPG) Block

## Anatomical Overview

| Structure | Location |
|-----------|----------|
| **Sphenopalatine Ganglion (SPG)** | Pterygopalatine fossa, posterior to middle turbinate |
| **Nasal cavity entry** | Via nares (nostrils) |
| **Key landmarks** | Inferior turbinate, Middle turbinate |

## Sphenopalatine Ganglion

### Anatomy
- Collection of nerve cell bodies in the **pterygopalatine fossa**
- **Posterior to middle turbinate**
- Yellow, branched structure in illustrations
- Largest ganglion outside the skull

### Innervation
- **Sensory**: Nasal cavity, palate, pharynx
- **Autonomic**: Parasympathetic to lacrimal and nasal glands
- **Associated nerves**: Maxillary nerve (V2), facial nerve branches

## SPG Block — Transnasal Technique

### Procedure
1. Patient supine with neck extended
2. Catheter/applicator inserted through **nares**
3. Pass over **inferior turbinate**
4. Pass **middle turbinate**
5. Reach **posterior nasal cavity** / pterygopalatine fossa
6. Deliver anesthetic (lidocaine, bupivacaine)

### Indications
| Condition | Use |
|-----------|-----|
| **Cluster headache** | Acute treatment and prevention |
| **Migraine** | Refractory cases |
| **SUNCT/SUNA** | Treatment option |
| **Facial pain** | Trigeminal neuralgia, atypical facial pain |
| **Status migrainosus** | Rescue therapy |

### Medications
- **Lidocaine** (4–5%)
- **Bupivacaine** (0.5%)
- Applied topically or via catheter

## Clinical Notes

- Non-invasive (no needles through skin)
- Can be performed at bedside
- Effect may last hours to weeks depending on technique
- Part of multi-modal headache management
