# Menopause Diagnosis

## Clinical Diagnosis

In women aged 45–55 with:
- 12 months amenorrhea
- Vasomotor symptoms (hot flashes, night sweats)

**→ No laboratory testing required.** This is a clinical diagnosis.

---

## When Labs Help

| Scenario | Why Test |
|---------|----------|
| Age <40 (suspected POI) | Confirms premature ovarian insufficiency |
| On hormonal contraception | Symptoms masked, no menstrual cue |
| Post-hysterectomy | No period to track |
| Atypical presentation | Rule out other causes |

---

## Laboratory Markers

| Marker | Menopausal Value | Notes |
|--------|------------------|-------|
| **FSH** | >40 IU/L | The workhorse — but fluctuates in perimenopause |
| **Estradiol (E2)** | <20–30 pg/mL | Low, confirms hypoestrogenism |
| **AMH** | Low/undetectable | Ovarian reserve marker; useful in POI workup |
| **LH** | Elevated | Less specific than FSH |

---

## Practical Tips

- **Timing**: Day 2–5 of cycle if still menstruating; any time if amenorrheic
- **Confirm POI**: FSH >40 on **two occasions, 4–6 weeks apart**
- **Perimenopause**: FSH is unreliable (fluctuates wildly) — clinical judgment trumps labs
- **TSH/prolactin**: Rule out thyroid dysfunction and hyperprolactinemia as causes of amenorrhea

---

## Bottom Line

Labs confirm what you already suspect. In classic presentation, they're optional. In younger women or atypical cases, FSH + E2 (± AMH) seals it.
