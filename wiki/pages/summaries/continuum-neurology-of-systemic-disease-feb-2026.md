# Continuum: Neurology of Systemic Disease — February 2026

**Citation:** Continuum (Minneap Minn) 2026;32(1, Neurology of Systemic Disease)
**Guest Editor:** Aaron L. Berkowitz, MD, PhD, FAAN

---

## Overview

This issue of Continuum covers neurologic complications arising from systemic medical diseases — organized by organ system. It includes 9 review articles, a selected topics piece on teaching neurology, and associated case-based content.

---

## Articles

### 1. Neurologic Consultation in Patients With Systemic Disease
**Author:** Aaron L. Berkowitz, MD, PhD, FAAN | pp. 13–21

Discusses pitfalls and diagnostic error-avoidance strategies when consulting on patients who develop neurologic symptoms in the setting of systemic disease. Covers attribution errors, anchoring, framing, and the importance of maintaining a broad differential before attributing findings to systemic disease alone.

### 2. Neurologic Manifestations of Cardiac Disease and Cardiac Procedures

- Stroke mechanisms in cardiac disease (large artery, small vessel, cardioembolic)
- Neurologic complications of cardiac surgery, catheterization, ECMO, LVAD

### 3. Neurologic Manifestations of Renal and Electrolyte Disorders
**Author:** Eelco F. M. Wijdicks, MD, PhD, FAAN, FACP, FNCS | pp. 57–74

**Key updates:**
- **Osmotic demyelination syndrome:** New evidence challenges the dogma that rapid correction of hyponatremia is causative. Retrospective studies of >22,000 patients show ODS is exceptionally rare (0.05%) and often occurs *without* rapid correction. Rapid correction may even improve outcomes.
- **Hyponatremia:** Brain adaptation occurs via initial cellular electrolyte loss (hours) followed by slower osmolyte extrusion (days). Symptoms correlate with both absolute level and rate of change.
- **Cefepime neurotoxicity:** Increasingly recognized in renal failure — can cause nonconvulsive status epilepticus.
- **Uremic encephalopathy:** Broad differential — PRES and subdural hematoma should be considered before attributing encephalopathy to uremia alone.

**Table highlights:**
- Correction formulas for sodium derangements
- Treatment tables for hypokalemia, hyperkalemia, hypophosphatemia, hyperphosphatemia, hypocalcemia, hypomagnesemia
- Hypernatremia correction: aim for 1–2 mmol/L/h reduction, avoid aggressive correction to normal (risk of hyponatremia)

### 4. Neurologic Manifestations of Hepatic and Gastrointestinal Disease
**Author:** Jessamyn Conell-Price, MD, MS | pp. 75–104

Covers:
- **Hepatic encephalopathy:** Covert (85% of cirrhosis) vs overt (30–50%); Animal Naming Test for screening; lactulose ± rifaximin for treatment
- **Acquired hepatocerebral degeneration:** Progressive nonfluctuating neuropsychiatric syndrome + movement disorders; symmetric T1 hyperintensity in globus pallidus on MRI; treat with liver transplant
- **Hepatic myelopathy:** Rare (2% of chronic liver disease); progressive spastic paraparesis, normal spine imaging
- **Wilson disease:** Autosomal recessive ATP7B mutation; neurologic symptoms may be presenting feature (~50% have no hepatic disease at onset); face of the giant panda sign on MRI; paradoxical neurologic worsening in 14–23% with treatment
- **Hepatitis C:** Universal screening recommended; 25% higher ischemic stroke risk; peripheral neuropathy (painful small fiber most common); cryoglobulinemia-associated vasculitis
- **Bariatric surgery complications:** Thiamine deficiency within 6 months; Wernicke encephalopathy risk; orthostatic hypotension in 4%
- **Celiac disease/gluten disorders:** Gluten ataxia (most common neurologic manifestation); up to 20% of sporadic ataxia; gluten neuropathy (2–8% of celiac)
- **Inflammatory bowel disease:** 50% increased MS risk; CVST risk (12/1000 annual incidence); TNF-α inhibitor demyelination risk (7.5/1000 person-years)
- **Whipple disease:** CSF PCR (69% sensitivity); oculomasticatory myorhythmia (pathognomonic); IRIS risk in first 3 months of treatment

### 5. Neurologic Manifestations of Endocrine Disorders
**Author:** Rafid Mustafa, MD | pp. 105–130

Covers neurologic complications of thyroid disorders (thyrotoxic periodic paralysis, hypothyroid myopathy/neuropathy), parathyroid disorders (hypercalcemic encephalopathy, hypocalcemic tetany), adrenal disorders, pituitary disorders, and diabetes mellitus (diabetic neuropathy, hypoglycemia-associated autonomic failure).

---

### 6. Neurologic Manifestations of Hematologic Disorders
**Authors:** Lauren Patrick, MD; Mark Terrelonge, MD, MPH | pp. 131–157

Reviews:
- Hypercoagulable states and stroke (antiphospholipid syndrome, thrombophilia)
- Sickle cell disease (stroke prevention with TCD screening + transfusion)
- Thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura (TTP) — neurologic involvement is common
- Coagulopathies and intracerebral hemorrhage
- Paraproteinemias and neuropathy (MGUS, POEMS, amyloidosis)
- Hematologic malignancies with neurologic involvement (CNS lymphoma, Lepto-meningeal disease)

---

### 7. Neurologic Manifestations of Rheumatologic Disease
**Author:** Shamik Bhattacharyya, MD, FAAN | pp. 159–189

Approach to diagnosis and treatment of neurologic syndromes in rheumatologic disease. Covers:
- SLE (CNS lupus, stroke, seizures, myelopathy)
- Rheumatoid arthritis (cervical myelopathy from C1-C2 instability, rheumatoid meningitis)
- Sjögren disease (neuropathy, CNS vasculitis)
- Sarcoidosis (neurosarcoidosis — cranial neuropathies, myelopathy, meningitis)
- ANCA-associated vasculitis, Behçet disease
- Central vein sign on MRI for MS vs vasculitic mimics

---

### 8. Neurologic Complications of Critical Illness
**Author:** Shivani Ghoshal, MD | pp. 191–209

Advances in screening, pathophysiology, and neuromonitoring. Covers:
- ICU-acquired weakness (CIM/CIP)
- Delirium in the ICU
- Sepsis-associated encephalopathy
- Neurologic prognostication after cardiac arrest
- Nonconvulsive seizures/status epilepticus in ICU
- Posterior reversible encephalopathy syndrome (PRES)

---

### 9. Neurologic Complications of Cancer and Its Treatment
**Author:** Amy A. Pruitt, MD, FAAN | pp. 211–241

Diagnosis and treatment of cancer-related neurologic complications:
- Brain, spinal, and leptomeningeal metastases
- Toxicities of cytotoxic, biologic, and targeted therapies
- Immune checkpoint inhibitor-associated neurologic adverse events
- Paraneoplastic neurologic syndromes
- Radiation-induced neurotoxicity (acute, early-delayed, late-delayed)

---

### 10. Neurologic Complications Associated With Pregnancy, Postpartum, and Menopause
**Author:** Sara C. LaHue, MD | pp. 243–275

Reviews neurologic complications in pregnancy (eclampsia, PRES, cerebral venous thrombosis, stroke), effect of pregnancy on preexisting neurologic disorders (epilepsy, MS, migraine, myasthenia gravis), and neurologic changes associated with menopause (migraine patterns, cognitive changes, stroke risk).

---

### 11. Neurologic Manifestations of Substance Use and Alcohol Use
**Author:** Adeline L. Goss, MD | pp. 277–308

Approach for identifying and managing substance use disorders. Covers:
- Alcohol-related: Wernicke encephalopathy, Marchiafava-Bignami disease, alcoholic cerebellar degeneration, peripheral neuropathy
- Opioids: overdose, myelopathy
- Stimulants: stroke (ischemic and hemorrhagic), vasculopathy
- Cannabis: hyperemesis syndrome, cognitive effects
- Hallucinogens, sedatives

---

### Selected Topics: Teaching Neurology in a Busy Clinical Practice
**Authors:** Mariah Lyn Robertson, MD, MPH; Rachel Marie E. Salas, MD, MEd, FAAN | pp. 310–325

Practical strategies for integrating teaching into high-volume clinical settings:
- One-Minute Preceptor and SNAPPS models for structured teaching
- Microteaching: 1–2 minute teaching pearls during downtime
- Prebriefing, goal setting, reflective practice
- Simulation-based training, blended learning, telemedicine
- Arts-based and humanities-based approaches to neurology education
- Gamification (board games, escape rooms)

## Key Clinical Pearls

| Topic | Key Takeaway |
|---|---|
| Hyponatremia correction | ODS is exceptionally rare (0.05%); rapid correction may be safer than feared |
| Uremic encephalopathy | Don't default to this diagnosis — consider PRES, cefepime toxicity, subdural hematoma |
| Wilson disease | Neurologic symptoms may be presenting feature; treat with slow chelation uptitration to avoid paradoxical worsening |
| HBV/HCV stroke risk | 25% higher ischemic stroke risk; universal screening recommended |
| TNF-α inhibitors | 2-fold risk of central demyelination; absolute risk 7.5/1000 person-years |
| IBD + MS | Natalizumab treats both; TNF-α inhibitors are contraindicated |
| IBD + CVST | 12/1000 annual incidence; anticoagulate even during flares |
| Bariatric surgery | Thiamine deficiency most common within 6 months; lifelong multivitamin monitoring |
| Cefepime in renal failure | Can cause nonconvulsive status epilepticus |
| Gluten ataxia | Up to 20% of sporadic ataxia |
| Whipple disease | Pathognomonic: oculomasticatory myorhythmia; CSF PCR 69% sensitive |
