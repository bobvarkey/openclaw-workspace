# Fibrillations

## Definition

Fibrillations are **spontaneous, rhythmic contractions of individual muscle fibers** that are visible on EMG or muscle ultrasound. They indicate **denervation** — muscle fibers losing their nerve supply.

## What Fibrillations Mean

- Muscle fibers are **denervated** (lost their nerve supply)
- Common in: motor neuron disease (ALS), radiculopathy, peripheral neuropathy
- A sign of **lower motor neuron** damage

## Detection Methods

| Method | How Detected |
|--------|-------------|
| **Needle EMG** | Spontaneous electrical activity at rest |
| **Muscle Ultrasound** | Visual micromovements of muscle fibers |

## EMG Characteristics

- **Polarity**: Positive sharp waves (initial positivity, then negativity)
- **Sound**: "Rain on a tin roof" — rapid, regular ticking
- **Rate**: Typically 0.5–10 Hz
- **Significance**: Indicates denervation when 2+ areas involved

## Clinical Context

| Condition | Fibrillations Present? |
|-----------|----------------------|
| ALS | Yes — common finding |
| Cervical/lumbar radiculopathy | Yes — in affected myotomes |
| Peripheral neuropathy | Yes — in severe cases |
| Myopathy | No (typically) |
| Guillain-Barré | Yes — early finding |

## Why This Matters

Fibrillations are one of the **key findings** that differentiate neurogenic from myopathic conditions. Their presence confirms ongoing denervation.
