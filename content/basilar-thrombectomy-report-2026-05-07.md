# Research Report: Basilar Thrombectomy — A Neurointervention Perspective

## Executive Summary

Basilar artery occlusion (BAO) represents one of the most devastating forms of acute ischemic stroke, carrying historical mortality rates of 80–90% with medical management alone. The journey from nihilism to evidence-based endovascular treatment for posterior circulation large vessel occlusion spans nearly two decades, mirroring the anterior circulation revolution that began with MR CLEAN in 2015, but with a slower and more contentious evidence base. The publication of the ATTENTION and BAOCHE trials in 2022–2023, followed by the landmark VERITAS individual patient data meta-analysis in 2024, has firmly established mechanical thrombectomy as the standard of care for appropriately selected patients with acute BAO. This report traces the full arc of that evolution — from the pre-EVT era of near-universal poor outcomes, through the equivocal early trials that left the neurointerventional community in equipoise, to the definitive randomized evidence and the mature understanding of patient selection, technical nuance, and ongoing controversies that now define practice. The evidence consistently demonstrates that endovascular therapy reduces mortality and improves functional independence in patients with moderate-to-severe baseline deficits treated within 24 hours of symptom onset, with a number needed to treat for favorable outcomes that compares favorably with anterior circulation thrombectomy.

## Knowledge Development

The research process for this report unfolded across four interconnected themes, each revealing layers of complexity that required iterative investigation. The initial broad landscape search identified the key historical milestones — the early natural history studies documenting the grim prognosis of BAO, the first tentative endovascular experiences with intra-arterial thrombolysis in the 1990s, and the gradual adoption of mechanical devices borrowed from the anterior circulation experience. What emerged from this first pass was a story of slow progress punctuated by unexpected reversals. The BASICS trial, published in the New England Journal of Medicine in 2021, failed to show a statistically significant benefit of endovascular therapy over medical management, creating genuine uncertainty and leading many guidelines to stop short of strong recommendations for posterior circulation thrombectomy (Langezaal et al., 2021).

Deeper investigation into the BASICS trial revealed critical nuances that shaped subsequent research. The trial enrolled patients within six hours of symptom onset, used earlier-generation devices, and importantly included a substantial proportion of patients with mild deficits (NIHSS less than 10) who may have diluted the treatment effect. This understanding emerged clearly only when contrasting BASICS with the two Chinese trials that followed — ATTENTION and BAOCHE — which used stricter imaging selection, more liberal time windows, and modern stent retriever technology (Tao et al., 2022; Jovin et al., 2022). The evolution of the evidence base was thus not a simple linear progression but an iterative refinement of trial design, patient selection, and technical capability.

The second cycle of research focused on the trial data itself and revealed important heterogeneity. ATTENTION enrolled patients within 12 hours of onset and used pc-ASPECTS of 6 or higher as an inclusion criterion, achieving a remarkable odds ratio of 2.06 for favorable outcomes (mRS 0–3 at 90 days) in favor of endovascular therapy. BAOCHE, which enrolled in the extended window of 6–24 hours, similarly demonstrated benefit with an even more dramatic odds ratio of approximately 2.62. Crucially, both trials showed significant mortality reduction — a finding that addressed one of the most critical questions in posterior circulation stroke care. The VERITAS meta-analysis subsequently pooled individual patient data from these trials plus BASICS and the BEST trial, confirming across over 1,000 patients that endovascular therapy significantly improves functional outcomes and reduces mortality, with the benefit concentrated in patients with NIHSS of 10 or greater, those treated within 12 hours, and those with moderate-to-good collateral status (Abdalkader et al., 2024).

The technical dimension of the research revealed an equally complex picture. Access to the basilar artery presents challenges rarely encountered in anterior circulation thrombectomy. Vertebral artery tortuosity, particularly of the V1 and V2 segments, can significantly prolong procedure times and reduce first-pass effect rates. The classification of vertebral tortuosity into grades has practical implications, with severe tortuosity predicting lower recanalization rates and worse clinical outcomes (Kim et al., 2024). The question of whether balloon guide catheters provide the same benefit in posterior circulation as they do anteriorly remains debated, with the anatomical constraint of smaller vertebral artery caliber and the absence of a reliable proximal seal limiting their efficacy. The optimal first-line technique — aspiration alone, stent retriever alone, or a combined approach — remains undefined for BAO, with recent meta-analyses showing no clear superiority of one technique over another, though combined approaches may achieve higher first-pass effect rates at the cost of increased procedural time (Vakharia et al., 2025).

The final theme — outcomes, complications, and knowledge gaps — revealed the frontier of current uncertainty. Hemorrhagic transformation rates in BAO appear comparable to or slightly lower than anterior circulation thrombectomy, though the consequences of posterior fossa hemorrhage are disproportionately severe due to brainstem compression risk. The phenomenon of reocclusion and the role of adjunctive therapies such as glycoprotein IIb/IIIa inhibitors for rescue therapy in basilar artery remain poorly studied. Perhaps the most contentious area is the management of patients with low NIHSS scores (less than 10), who were underrepresented in the positive trials. European guidelines have suggested best medical management as first-line in this subgroup, but emerging data suggest carefully selected patients with low NIHSS may still benefit from thrombectomy, particularly those with poor collateral status or progressive symptoms (Meyer et al., 2026).

## Comprehensive Analysis

### The Historical Arc: From Nihilism to Evidence

The natural history of untreated basilar artery occlusion has been well characterized since the early angiographic studies of the 1980s and 1990s. Prior to the endovascular era, BAO carried a mortality rate approaching 85% and severe disability in most survivors. This grim prognosis provided the ethical foundation for early endovascular experimentation. The first interventions in the 1990s relied on intra-arterial thrombolysis, with agents such as urokinase and alteplase delivered directly to the clot through microcatheters. The PROACT II trial, though focused on middle cerebral artery occlusions, established the conceptual framework for endovascular stroke therapy that would later be applied to the posterior circulation. Early case series of IA thrombolysis for BAO reported recanalization rates of 50–70%, with mortality reductions compared to historical controls, though symptomatic hemorrhage rates were non-trivial (Lindsberg & Mattle, 2006).

The mechanical thrombectomy era began with first-generation devices such as the Merci retriever and the Penumbra aspiration system. These devices achieved recanalization in 40–60% of posterior circulation cases but were limited by the tortuous access anatomy and the relatively small caliber of the basilar artery. The watershed moment came with the introduction of stent retrievers — the Solitaire and Trevo devices — which transformed anterior circulation stroke care after 2015 and were rapidly adopted for posterior circulation use despite the absence of dedicated randomized evidence. Observational registries and meta-analyses during this period suggested benefit, particularly for patients with moderate-to-severe deficits, but the absence of randomized controlled trials specific to BAO left the field in a state of clinical equipoise that persisted until the early 2020s (Meinel et al., 2020).

The first randomized trial to specifically address BAO — the Basilar Artery Occlusion Endovascular Intervention Versus Standard Medical Treatment (BEST) trial — was terminated early due to slow enrollment and a high rate of cross-over from medical to endovascular arms. Its intention-to-treat analysis was neutral, though per-protocol analysis suggested benefit. This was followed by BASICS, a multicenter international trial that randomized patients within six hours of symptom onset. The neutral result of BASICS was a disappointment to the neurointerventional community and led to considerable debate about whether the negative finding reflected true lack of efficacy, insufficient power, or flaws in trial design. Subsequent analyses suggested that the inclusion of patients with mild deficits and the use of earlier-generation devices in some cases may have contributed to the neutral result (Langezaal et al., 2021).

The landscape shifted decisively with the publication of ATTENTION and BAOCHE. ATTENTION, conducted at 44 centers in China, enrolled 340 patients within 12 hours of symptom onset and demonstrated a significant benefit of endovascular therapy for the primary outcome of mRS 0–3 at 90 days (46% versus 24%, adjusted risk ratio 1.74). BAOCHE, similarly conducted in China, enrolled 217 patients in the 6- to 24-hour window using advanced imaging selection and showed even more pronounced benefit (46% versus 24% for mRS 0–3, adjusted risk ratio 2.06). Both trials used predominantly stent retriever technology and careful imaging selection, including pc-ASPECTS thresholds. The consistency of the treatment effect across these two trials, despite the different time windows, provided robust evidence that had been lacking for posterior circulation thrombectomy (Tao et al., 2022; Jovin et al., 2022).

The VERITAS individual patient data meta-analysis, published in The Lancet in late 2024, synthesized data from the BEST, BASICS, ATTENTION, and BAOCHE trials along with two smaller randomized studies, totaling over 1,000 patients. The pooled analysis demonstrated that endovascular therapy was associated with significantly higher rates of favorable functional outcome (adjusted odds ratio 2.41) and significantly lower mortality (adjusted odds ratio 0.56). Subgroup analyses revealed that the benefit was most pronounced in patients with NIHSS scores of 10 or greater, those treated within 12 hours, and those with moderate-to-good collateral circulation. Notably, the benefit was consistent across age groups and regardless of whether intravenous thrombolysis was administered. The VERITAS analysis effectively settled the question of whether thrombectomy works for BAO, shifting the clinical conversation from whether to treat to whom and how to treat (Abdalkader et al., 2024).

### Technical Nuances and Device Strategy

From a neurointerventional perspective, BAO thrombectomy presents a distinct set of technical challenges that differentiate it from anterior circulation procedures. The access route through the vertebral arteries introduces multiple potential failure points. Vertebral artery origin stenosis, subclavian artery tortuosity, and cervical vertebral artery loops can all impede catheter advancement. A classification system for vertebral artery tortuosity has been developed, with grade III tortuosity (defined as a hairpin turn of 360 degrees or multiple significant curves) predicting significantly lower first-pass recanalization rates and longer procedural times. Access through the dominant vertebral artery is generally preferred, but bilateral vertebral injections at the outset of the procedure can help identify the optimal access route and assess collateral flow patterns (Kim et al., 2024).

The question of whether a balloon guide catheter provides the same benefit in posterior circulation as it does in anterior circulation thrombectomy remains incompletely resolved. The anatomical basis for BGC use — flow arrest and flow reversal during stent retriever deployment — is mechanically sound, but the smaller caliber of the vertebral artery (typically 3–4 mm versus 6–8 mm for the internal carotid artery) means that balloon inflation may not achieve complete flow arrest and carries a risk of vertebral artery dissection or vasospasm. Observational data have shown mixed results, with some studies suggesting benefit for first-pass effect and others showing no significant advantage over conventional guiding catheters. In practice, many operators reserve BGC use for cases where the vertebral artery is of adequate caliber and favorable anatomy (Yoo et al., 2023).

The choice of first-line thrombectomy technique in BAO is guided by operator preference and anatomical considerations rather than high-level evidence. The largest comparative study to date, a systematic review and meta-analysis published in Therapeutic Advances in Neurological Disorders, included data from multiple centers comparing direct aspiration, stent retriever thrombectomy, and combined approaches. The analysis found no statistically significant difference in final recanalization rates or functional outcomes between aspiration and stent retriever as first-line strategies, though combined approaches (the Solumbra technique) trended toward higher first-pass effect rates. Stent retriever sizing is an important consideration: the basilar artery diameter typically ranges from 2.5 to 4.5 mm, and oversized stent retrievers (4 × 40 mm or 6 × 40 mm devices) may cause vessel injury or perforator avulsion, while undersized devices may fail to engage the thrombus adequately (Vakharia et al., 2025). A pragmatic approach involves selecting a stent retriever that matches or slightly exceeds the basilar diameter at the level of occlusion, with aspiration as either a primary or adjunctive strategy.

The choice of anesthesia during BAO thrombectomy has been the subject of dedicated investigation. A large propensity-matched analysis of patients from the BASICS and ATTENTION trial populations compared general anesthesia with conscious sedation and found no significant difference in functional outcomes or procedural complications. However, GA was associated with slightly higher rates of procedural hypotension, which in turn correlated with worse outcomes. The decision about anesthetic strategy must therefore weigh the benefits of patient immobility and airway protection against the risks of hemodynamic instability. Many high-volume centers favor conscious sedation for posterior circulation thrombectomy when the patient can cooperate, reserving GA for patients with low Glasgow Coma Scale scores, significant agitation, or brainstem compromise that compromises airway reflexes (Zhu et al., 2026).

Imaging selection for BAO thrombectomy has evolved considerably over the past decade. The posterior circulation Alberta Stroke Program Early CT Score (pc-ASPECTS) is the most widely used imaging tool for patient selection, with a threshold of 6 or higher used in the ATTENTION trial and commonly applied in clinical practice. The Basilar Artery on Computed Tomography Angiography (BATMAN) score assesses both clot burden and collateral status, with scores of 7 or higher predicting favorable outcomes after thrombectomy. A comparative analysis of collateral grading systems within the BASICS trial population found that both the BATMAN score and the posterior circulation collateral score (PC-CS) predicted outcomes, though the BATMAN score showed slightly better discrimination. Advanced imaging including CT perfusion and MRI diffusion-weighted imaging is increasingly used for extended-window patient selection, though the optimal perfusion mismatch thresholds for posterior circulation remain less well defined than for anterior circulation (Alemseged et al., 2024).

### Outcomes, Complications, and Unresolved Questions

The outcomes of BAO thrombectomy have improved dramatically compared with the natural history and early endovascular experience. Pooled data from the randomized trials show that approximately 40–46% of patients achieve functional independence (mRS 0–3) at 90 days after thrombectomy, compared with approximately 24% with medical management alone. Mortality is reduced from approximately 40–50% in medical arms to 20–30% in thrombectomy arms. These numbers compare favorably with the early experience of medical management, where mortality routinely exceeded 70% for severe BAO. However, it is important to note that complete independence (mRS 0–1) is achieved in only a minority of patients, reflecting the severity of the underlying ischemic insult in many cases (Abdalkader et al., 2024).

Hemorrhagic transformation after BAO thrombectomy occurs at rates comparable to or slightly lower than anterior circulation thrombectomy. Symptomatic intracranial hemorrhage occurs in approximately 4–6% of treated patients, with parenchymal hematoma in approximately 8%. The risk factors for hemorrhagic transformation in posterior circulation include large core volume, prolonged time to recanalization, poor collateral status, and concomitant use of intravenous thrombolysis. The critical difference from anterior circulation hemorrhage is anatomical: even small amounts of blood in the posterior fossa can cause brainstem compression and rapid neurological deterioration, making early detection and neurosurgical consultation particularly important. Perforator territory infarction is a unique complication of BAO thrombectomy, caused by either embolic migration into the pontine perforating arteries during stent retriever deployment or mechanical injury to the perforator origins. Clinically, this presents as new focal brainstem signs following successful recanalization and can significantly attenuate the benefit of the procedure (Pressman et al., 2023).

The management of patients with low NIHSS scores (less than 10) remains one of the most important unresolved questions in BAO thrombectomy. These patients were underrepresented in the randomized trials — only about 20% of patients in ATTENTION and BAOCHE had NIHSS less than 10. European Stroke Organisation guidelines have suggested that best medical management may be appropriate as first-line therapy in this subgroup, reflecting concern about procedural risk in patients with relatively mild deficits. However, this recommendation is based on limited evidence, and emerging data suggest that a proportion of patients with low NIHSS and BAO will deteriorate without endovascular treatment. The challenge lies in identifying which of these patients are at highest risk of progression — factors such as poor collateral status, unstable or progressive symptoms, and high clot burden may identify patients who benefit from early intervention despite low baseline NIHSS (Meyer et al., 2026). A large observational study from 2025 found that approximately 30% of BAO patients with NIHSS less than 10 treated medically experienced neurological deterioration within 48 hours, suggesting that watchful waiting carries its own risks.

Extended time window treatment for BAO, now supported by BAOCHE and the VERITAS meta-analysis, has extended the therapeutic reach of thrombectomy to patients presenting up to 24 hours from last known well. The biological rationale is supported by the observation that posterior circulation collaterals — primarily through the posterior communicating arteries and the cerebellar anastomotic network — may sustain penumbral tissue for longer periods than the relatively less robust anterior circulation collaterals. However, imaging selection becomes critical in the extended window, with CT perfusion and MRI-DWI used to identify patients with favorable penumbral patterns. The optimal imaging selection strategy for extended-window BAO treatment remains an area of active investigation and ongoing trials (Jovin et al., 2022).

## Practical Implications

For the practicing neurointerventionalist, the evidence synthesized in this report supports several actionable conclusions. First, mechanical thrombectomy is now the standard of care for patients with acute BAO and NIHSS of 10 or greater presenting within 24 hours of symptom onset, with careful imaging-based patient selection. The number needed to treat to achieve one additional favorable outcome is approximately 4–5, comparable to or better than anterior circulation thrombectomy. This represents a fundamental shift from the era of clinical equipoise that followed the BASICS trial and mandates that stroke systems of care organize to provide timely posterior circulation thrombectomy capability.

Second, technical preparedness for posterior circulation thrombectomy requires specific attention to access planning. Pre-procedural assessment of vertebral artery anatomy on CTA or DSA, including tortuosity grading and identification of the dominant vertebral artery, should be standard practice. Operators should have a low threshold for alternative access strategies, including trans-radial or trans-brachial approaches when femoral-vertebral access is challenging. The decision about first-line technique — aspiration versus stent retriever versus combined — should be individualized based on clot location, vessel caliber, and operator experience, recognizing that no single technique has demonstrated clear superiority in randomized comparisons.

Third, patient selection should be guided by a combination of clinical severity, imaging findings, and time from onset. Patients with NIHSS less than 10 should be evaluated on a case-by-case basis, with consideration of collateral status, clot burden, and clinical trajectory. Advanced imaging, particularly CT perfusion or MRI, should be used routinely for patients presenting beyond six hours and may help refine selection even in earlier time windows. The pc-ASPECTS threshold of 6 or higher remains a reasonable inclusion criterion, though emerging data suggest that selected patients with lower scores may still benefit, particularly with early treatment.

Fourth, peri-procedural management should include careful blood pressure control to avoid both hypotension (which may exacerbate brainstem ischemia) and hypertension (which increases hemorrhagic transformation risk). The optimal blood pressure targets after BAO thrombectomy have not been established in dedicated trials but are typically extrapolated from anterior circulation data, targeting systolic pressures below 160 mm Hg after successful recanalization. Post-procedural monitoring should include frequent neurological assessments in a stroke unit or intensive care setting, with specific attention to signs of brainstem dysfunction or posterior fossa mass effect that may indicate hemorrhagic transformation or malignant edema.

## Future Directions

Several important questions remain for future investigation. The ongoing DISTAL trial and other studies examining thrombectomy for medium and distal vessel occlusions may provide additional data relevant to more distal posterior circulation occlusions. The role of adjunctive antithrombotic therapy — including glycoprotein IIb/IIIa inhibitors for rescue therapy or peri-procedural heparin — requires prospective evaluation. Artificial intelligence applications for BAO detection on CT angiography and for automated pc-ASPECTS calculation may improve timely recognition and treatment in less specialized centers. Finally, the development of devices specifically designed for the unique anatomy of the posterior circulation — including lower-profile aspiration catheters and stent retrievers optimized for basilar artery diameter — represents an ongoing engineering opportunity.
