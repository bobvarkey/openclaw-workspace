# Research Report: Flow Diverters for Intracranial Aneurysms — A Neurointervention Perspective

## Executive Summary

Flow diversion has fundamentally transformed the endovascular treatment of intracranial aneurysms over the past decade and a half, shifting the paradigm from intrasaccular coil embolization to parent vessel remodeling as the primary therapeutic mechanism. The Pipeline Embolization Device (PED), first approved by the FDA in 2011 for the treatment of large and giant wide-necked aneurysms of the internal carotid artery, established the foundation for a new era in aneurysm treatment, and subsequent device evolution has expanded the therapeutic reach to include smaller aneurysms, posterior circulation lesions, and increasingly complex morphologies. The evidence base has matured substantially, with long-term follow-up data from the PUFS trial demonstrating complete occlusion rates of 95.2% at 5 years and major stroke or neurologic death rates of 5.6%, establishing flow diversion as a durable and relatively safe treatment option for complex aneurysms that were previously considered untreatable or requiring open surgical approaches.

The field has evolved considerably from the first-generation Pipeline device to include multiple competitor platforms — including the Silk (Balt), Surpass Streamline and Evolve (Stryker), FRED-X (MicroVention), p64 and p48 (phenox), Tubridge (MicroPort), and Derivo (Acandis) — each with distinct design philosophies, mesh densities, and delivery systems. The most significant recent advancement has been the development of surface-modified devices, particularly the Pipeline Shield (phosphorylcholine coating) and Surpass Evolve, which have demonstrated reduced thrombogenicity and improved safety profiles without sacrificing occlusion efficacy. Current practice guidelines from major societies, including the American Heart Association/Stroke Association and the European Stroke Organisation, now strongly recommend flow diversion as a first-line treatment option for large and giant aneurysms of the internal carotid artery, with expanding recommendations for smaller aneurysms, bifurcation lesions, and select posterior circulation aneurysms.

The evidence base continues to evolve with the PREMIER trial expanding indications to small and medium aneurysms, multiple registries providing real-world outcomes data, and ongoing investigation into bioresorbable flow diverters and drug-eluting surfaces representing the next frontier in this rapidly advancing field. This report synthesizes the full arc of flow diversion from its origins through current best practices to future directions, providing a comprehensive neurointervention perspective on one of the most important technological advances in cerebrovascular therapy.

## Knowledge Development

The research process for this report encompassed four interconnected themes, each requiring iterative investigation to build a coherent understanding. The initial landscape search traced flow diversion's origins to the pre-device era, when aneurysm treatment relied primarily on surgical clipping and, beginning in 1990, the Guglielmi detachable coil (GDC) system. The ISAT trial, published in the early 2000s, established the superiority of coiling over clipping for ruptured aneurysms in terms of survival benefit, but the fundamental limitation of coiling emerged clearly: wide-necked aneurysms, large and giant lesions, and those with unfavorable dome-to-neck ratios remained challenging to treat with coils alone, carrying high recurrence rates that frequently necessitated repeat treatment. Balloon-assisted coiling and, subsequently, stent-assisted coiling partially addressed these limitations by providing scaffold support for coil pack- ing in wide-necked lesions, but these approaches fundamentally remained focused on intrasaccular embolization rather than parent vessel remodeling. The theoretical foundation for flow diversion emerged from the recognition that aneurysm obliteration could be achieved not by Filling the aneurysm sac but by redirecting blood flow away from the aneurysm ostium, promoting spontaneous thrombosis and endothelialization across the aneurysm neck. This conceptual shift — from filling the aneurysm to reconstructing the parent vessel — represented perhaps the most important paradigm change in aneurysm treatment since the introduction of coiling itself.

The first clinical use of flow diverters occurred in Europe with the Silk device (Balt) beginning in 2006, followed by the Pipeline Embolization Device in clinical trials starting in 2008. The pivotal PUFS (Pipeline for Uncoilable or Failed Aneurysms) trial, published in Radiology in 2013, enrolled 108 patients with large and giant wide-necked aneurysms of the internal carotid artery that were either uncoilable or had failed previous coiling attempts, demonstrating a complete occlusion rate of 73.6% at 6 months and 86.8% at 1 year, rising to 93.4% at 3 years and 95.2% at 5 years follow-up. Critically, the major stroke or neurologic death rate was 5.6%, establishing an acceptable safety profile for what had previously been considered untreatable pathology.

The second cycle of research focused on indications expansion. The PREMIER trial, published in the Journal of NeuroInterventional Surgery in 2021, expanded the evidence base to small and medium aneurysms (up to 12mm), demonstrating complete occlusion in 76.8% at 6 months and 87.4% at 1 year, with remarkably low combined major morbidity and mortality of 2.1%. This trial was practice-changing, establishing flow diversion as not merely a salvage option for lesions that failed coiling but a first-line treatment option across a broader spectrum of aneurysm sizes. The PREMIER 3-year follow-up data confirmed durability, with no cases of aneurysm rupture and continued progressive occlusion, confirming the long-term safety and efficacy of flow diversion for small and medium unruptured intracranial aneurysms.

Posterior circulation aneurysms presented distinct challenges that required dedicated investigation. The basilar artery, particularly the perforator-rich trunk segment, posed unique risks of perforator infarction not seen in anterior circulation treatment. A systematic review and meta-analysis published in Frontiers in Neurology in 2025 found that flow diversion for posterior circulation aneurysms achieved lower complete occlusion rates than anterior circulation lesions but with acceptable safety profiles when performed at high-volume centers. The risk of perforator infarction — wherein flow diversion comprom- ises the ostia of branch vessels arising from the covered segment — emerged as the most significant complication specific to posterior circulation use, particularly for aneurysms involving the basilar trunk between the anterior inferior cerebellar artery (AICA) and superior cerebellar artery (SCA) segments.

The third theme — technical and procedural considerations — revealed the complexity of deploying flow diverters effectively. Antiplatelet therapy emerged as the cornerstone of safe flow diversion, with dual antiplatelet therapy (DAPT) using aspirin combined with clopidogrel becoming the standard regimen. However, clopidogrel resistance, affecting an estimated 20-30% of patients, has driven increasing use of platelet function testing (VerifyNow) and genetic testing (CYP2C19 polymorphisms) to identify poor metabolizers. The SAFEST study, surveying 442 practitioners from 53 countries, found that 67% used aspirin plus clopidogrel, but alternative P2Y12 inhibitors were increasingly employed, with 21% preferring ticagrelor and 10% preferring prasugrel, driven primarily by concerns about clopidogrel variable responsiveness and the desire for more potent platelet inhibition. A 2026 publication from the Journal of NeuroInterventional Surgery described institutional adoption of universal prasugrel-based DAPT, representing a significant practice shift from empiric clopidogrel to genotype-guided or universally potent antiplatelet therapy.

Deployment technique has evolved considerably since the first Pipeline deployments. Contemporary practice emphasizes meticulous device sizing — typically oversizing the flow diverter 0.5-1.0mm beyond the parent vessel diameter to ensure wall apposition — and careful attention to landing zones, with adequate proximal and distal segments to ensure complete aneurysm neck coverage. The choice of microcatheter has standardized around the Marksman (Medtronic), Phenom (Medtronic), and Headway (MicroVention) platforms, deployed over 0.014-inch microwires including the Synchro (Stryker) and Traxcess (MicroVention). The unsheathing technique has largely replaced push- pull deployment, with gradual deployment allowing the device to open progressively and minimizing the risk of jumping or misplacement.

The fourth theme — complications and future directions — revealed the ongoing challenges. Delayed aneurysm rupture (DAR), wherein a previously stable aneurysm ruptures days to weeks after flow diverter deployment, remains one of the most feared complications, with unclear mechanisms but hypothesized relationships to intra-aneurysmal pressure changes during thrombosis and the risk being higher in giant aneurysms. A 2025 meta-analysis in the Journal of NeuroInterventional Surgery systematically reviewed DAR incidence and identified predictive factors, establishing the incidence at approximately 1-2% overall but significantly higher in giant aneurysms. Other complications include in-stent thrombosis (particularly in patients with inadequate antiplatelet therapy), perforator infarction (particularly in posterior circulation), distal emboli from device-related thrombosis, and parent vessel stenosis (more common with first-generation devices).

Future directions are particularly exciting. Bioresorbable flow diverters, using magnesium-based or polymer scaffolds, aim to provide temporary flow diversion while scaffold resorption avoids the long-term risks of permanent metal implants. A 2026 systematic review identified several bioresorbable platforms in preclinical and early clinical development, though efficacy and safety data remain preliminary. Drug-eluting surfaces, incorporating anti-proliferative agents to accelerate endothelialization and reduce thrombosis risk, represent another active research area. AI-assisted sizing, using computational fluid dynamics modeling to predict flow diverter behavior before deployment, may eventually personalize device selection for individual patients.

## Comprehensive Analysis

### The Historical Arc: From Coiling to Flow Diversion

The treatment of intracranial aneurysms has evolved through distinct eras, each defined by a dominant technological approach. The surgical era, beginning in the 1930s with the first clip ligation procedures, established the foundational principle that aneurysm exclusion from the circulation prevented rerupture. The introduction of the Guglielmi detachable coil in 1990 initiated the endovascular revolution, and the ISAT trial subsequently established coil embolization as superior to clipping for ruptured aneurysms in terms of survival benefit at 1 year, driving widespread adoption of endovascular techniques. However, even as coiling became the dominant treatment modality, certain aneurysm subtypes remained stubbornly difficult to treat. Wide-necked aneurysms (typically defined as neck width 4mm or greater or dome-to-neck ratio less than 2) posed particular challenges, as coils tended to prolapse into the parent vessel or fail to achieve stable pack- ing density. Large and giant aneurysms (defined as 10mm or greater and 25mm or greater, respectively) carried high recurrence rates following coiling, with some series reporting recurrence rates exceeding 30-40% at follow-up. These morphological challenges drove the development of adjunctive techniques, including balloon-assisted coiling (which used temporary balloon inflation to protect the parent vessel during coil deployment) and stent-assisted coiling (which provided a permanent scaffold to support coil packing while also preventing coil prolapse).

These adjuncts, while improving the treating of wide-necked aneurysms, did not fundamentally alter the treatment paradigm. Coiling remained an intrasaccular technique — filling the aneurysm sac with platinum coils to promote thrombosis — and the long-term durability depended on coil density and complete angiographic occlusion. Recurrence, driven by compaction of coil masses as the aneurysm thrombosis organized and retracted, remained an inherent limitation, particularly for large and large aneurysms, driving the need for retreatment in a significant proportion of cases.

Flow diversion represented a conceptual shift of extraordinary significance. Rather than filling the aneurysm, flow diversion worked by placing a closely woven mesh stent across the aneurysm neck to redirect blood flow away from the aneurysm sac, promoting spontaneous thrombosis while maintaining parent vessel patency. The first flow diverter, the Silk (Balt), was introduced in clinical practice in Europe in 2006, followed by the Pipeline Embolization Device (Medtronic) in clinical trials beginning in 2008. The fundamental difference from stents used in stent-assisted coiling was mesh density — flow diverters had pore sizes of 25-35 threads per square inch (compared to approximately 8-12 for standard intracranial stents), creating a mechanical barrier to aneurysm inflow while still permitting branch vessel and perforator patency. This distinction — between a scaffold for coils and a flow redirecting device — was so significant that the FDA classified flow diverters as a new device category rather than a variation of existing stents.

The PUFS trial, published in 2013 and subsequently updated with 5-year follow-up data, established the efficacy and safety profile that would define the field. Among 108 patients with large and giant (mean dome size 18.8mm) wide-necked aneurysms of the internal carotid artery, complete or near-complete occlusion was achieved in 73.6% at 6 months, 86.8% at 1 year, 93.4% at 3 years, and 95.2% at 5 years. The combined rate of major stroke or neurologic death was 5.6%, remarkably low considering the complexity and size of the treated aneurysms. These results were superior to historical outcomes with coiling or surgical clipping for analogous lesions and established flow diversion as the treatment of choice for large and giant ICA aneurysms.

Subsequent trials and registries refined the evidence base. The PREMIER trial extended indications to small and medium aneurysms, demonstrating complete occlusion in 76.8% at 6 months and 87.4% at 1 year, with combined major morbidity and mortality of only 2.1% — lower than PUFS, reflecting both operator learning curve improvements and the generally less challenging anatomy of smaller lesions. The PREMIER 3-year results confirmed durability, with continued progressive occlusion and no delayed ruptures. The IntrePED registry, a real-world multicenter registry including over 1,000 patients, corroborated the clinical trial data, demonstrating overall complete occlusion rates and major complication rates consistent with the pivotal trials.

### Indications: Where Flow Diversion Reigns

The approved and established indications for flow diversion have expanded substantially since the original FDA approval in 2011, which was limited to large and giant wide-necked aneurysms of the internal carotid artery. This expansion reflects both the accumulating evidence base and the development of newer-generation devices with improved safety profiles.

Large and giant ICA aneurysms remain the classic and most established indication. The PUFS trial and subsequent registries have consistently demonstrated high complete occlusion rates (approximately 75-80% at 6 months, rising to over 90% at 3-5 years) with acceptable morbidity (approximately 5-8%). These lesions, which were frequently untreatable or required high-risk surgical approaches before flow diversion, are now routinely treated endovascularly with flow diverters. The evidence is mature and the recommendation is strong, supported by both FDA approval and major society guidelines.

Small and medium aneurysms represent the most significant indication expansion, supported by the PREMIER trial. Aneurysms up to 12mm in maximum diameter showed complete occlusion rates of 76.8% at 6 months and 87.4% at 1 year, with remarkably low morbidity of 2.1%. The implications of this trial were substantial — flow diversion became a viable first-line treatment option not merely for complex, large lesions but for the much larger population of small-to-medium saccular aneurysms that had previously been treated with coiling. Flow diversion offered superior long-term durability compared to coiling — with progressive occlusion over time rather than the recurrence risk associated with coil compaction — without significantly higher morbidity, making it an attractive option for young patients with long life expectancies.

Bifurcation aneurysms, including middle cerebral artery (MCA) bifurcation, anterior communicating artery (Acom), and posterior communicating artery (Pcom) aneurysms, present distinct challenges for flow diversion, as the device must preserve flow to branch vessels while occluding the aneurysm. The intrasaccular web device (WEB, MicroVention), sometimes considered a flow disruptor rather than flow diverter, has emerged as an alternative for bifurcation aneurysms, providing intrasaccular flow disruption while preserving parent vessel branch patency without requiring antiplatelet therapy. Comparative data are limited, but the choice between flow diversion and WEB for bifurcation aneurysms is increasingly individualized based on anatomy, patient characteristics, and operator preference.

Posterior circulation aneurysms remain controversial. The basilar artery trunk, with its dense perforating vessel network supplying the brainstem, poses unique risks that are not present in ICA treatment. Perforator infarction — injury to branch vessels arising from the covered segment — is the most significant complication, with published series reporting rates of 2-8% depending on aneurysm location and device selection. The 2025 meta-analysis in Frontiers in Neurology found lower complete occlusion rates compared to anterior circulation but acceptable safety at high-volume centers. Most operators exercise caution with basilar trunk aneurysms, frequently reserving flow diversion for lesions that are not amenable to alternative approaches.

Ruptured aneurysms in the acute setting represent an area of active investigation. The need for dual antiplatelet therapy — which would be contraindicated in the setting of active subarachnoid hemorrhage with potential need for surgical intervention — has limited the use of flow diversion in acute rupture. However, some series have reported successful flow diversion for ruptured blister aneurysms and dissecting pseudoaneurysms, which are particularly challenging to treat with coiling. The evidence is limited and largely restricted to case series, but some operators have adopted flow diversion as a salvage approach for ruptured lesions that are not amenable to coiling or surgical approaches.

### Technical and Procedural Considerations

The technical success of flow diversion depends on several procedural factors, from patient selection through antiplatelet preparation to device deployment and follow-up. The mastery of these factors distinguishes experienced flow diversion operators and contributes to the outcomes achieved in published series.

Antiplatelet therapy is fundamental to safe flow diversion. The flow diverter, as a permanent metallic implant, creates a thrombogenic surface that requires continuous antiplatelet inhibition to prevent in-stent thrombosis and downstream embolic complications. Dual antiplatelet therapy (DAPT), typically combining aspirin with a P2Y12 inhibitor, is the standard of care. Clopidogrel (Plavix), a prodrug requiring hepatic metabolism to the active form, has been the most widely used P2Y12 inhibitor but has well-documented variable responsiveness — an estimated 20-30% of patients demonstrate high platelet reactivity despite standard dosing, putting them at increased risk of thrombotic complications. The identification of clopidogrel non-responders has driven the adoption of platelet function testing (VerifyNow P2Y12 assay) and genetic testing (CYP2C19 genotype) to guide therapy selection.

More potent P2Y12 inhibitors have gained adoption. Ticagrelor (Brilinta), a reversible direct-acting P2Y12 inhibitor with more predictable pharmacodynamics, has been increasingly used, with some centers adopting universal ticagregrel-based DAPT. Prasugrel, another prodrug with more efficient activation than clopidogrel, has also been adopted, with a 2026 publication describing institutional adoption of universal prasugrel-based DAPT as standard practice. The SAFEST survey found that 67% of international practitioners continued to use aspirin plus clopidogrel, but with 21% using ticagrelor and 10% using prasugrel, reflecting the trend toward more potent agents or genotype-guided selection.

Device deployment technique has evolved since the first Pipeline procedures. Contemporary practice emphasizes careful sizing — typically oversizing the device 0.5-1.0mm beyond the measured parent vessel diameter to ensure wall apposition and minimize the risk of endoleak (persistent aneurysm filling around the device). Landing zone assessment is critical, with adequate lengths of normal vessel both proximal and distal to the aneurysm neck to ensure complete coverage. The microcatheter selection (Marksman, Phenom, or Headway) is typically operator-dependent, with the choice influenced by vessel anatomy and personal experience.

The unsheathing technique has largely replaced push-pull deployment, allowing gradual device opening and minimizing the risk of device jumping or migration. Careful attention to wire position and microcatheter control during deployment prevents inadvertent advancement that could cause parent vessel injury or aneurysm perforation. Post-deployment angiography confirms wall apposition and identifies any residual inflow channels, while cone-beam CT can detect incomplete apposition that might not be apparent on conventional angiography.

Follow-up protocols have standardized around digital subtraction angiography at 6 months, 1 year, and 2-3 years post-procedure, with MRA (particularly contrast-enhanced MRA) as an alternative for patients who cannot undergo catheter angiography. The progressive nature of flow diversion — with occlusion rates increasing over time rather than declining, as in coiling — has led to the recognition that early follow-up may underestimate final occlusion rates, and many centers have extended follow-up to 5 years or beyond.

Complications from flow diversion, while uncommon, are well-characterized. In-stent thrombosis, typically presenting within the first weeks post-procedure, is the most common serious complication and is almost universally associated with inadequate antiplatelet therapy or unexpected clopidogrel non-responsiveness. Delayed aneurysm rupture, presenting days to weeks post-procedure, is poorly understood but is hypothesized to relate to intra-aneurysmal pressure changes during thrombosis, with giant aneurysms carrying the highest risk. Perforator infarction is specific to posterior circulation treatment and results from compromise of branch vessel ostia. Parenchymal hemorrhage, presenting with new intracranial hemorrhage remote from the treated aneurysm, is rare but has been reported, potentially related to hemorrhage from embolic complications or, in rare cases, a hemorrhagic complication of flow diversion itself.

### Outcomes and Future Directions

The outcomes of flow diversion have been characterized by progressive occlusion over time rather than the recurrence seen with coiling. The PUFS 5-year data showed occlusion rates continuing to increase from 73.6% at 6 months to 95.2% at 5 years, reflecting the progressive endothelialization and aneurysm neck remodeling achieved by flow diversion. This durability — unique among aneurysm treatments — represents one of the most significant advantages of flow diversion, particularly for young patients with long life expectancies who might otherwise require multiple retreatments over their lifetimes.

Retreatment rates, while lower than with coiling, are not zero. The progressive occlusion seen with flow diversion is not always complete, and some aneurysms demonstrate persistent inflow or recanalization despite initial flow diversion. Adjunctive coiling, particularly for large and giant aneurysms at high risk of delayed rupture, has been adopted by many operators to accelerate sac thrombosis and reduce the risk of this devastating complication. The combination of flow diversion with adjunctive intrasaccular devices (coils or WEB) represents an area of active investigation.

The comparison between flow diversion and flow disruption (with the WEB device) has emerged as an important clinical question. WEB provides intrasaccular flow disruption through a meshed sphere deployed within the aneurysm sac, with theoretical advantages including the absence of required antiplatelet therapy (as there is no parent vessel implant) and preservation of branch vessel patency without the perforator infarction risk of flow diversion. However, WEB has not demonstrated superior occlusion rates compared to flow diversion in head-to-head comparisons, and the choice between approaches is increasingly individualized.

The future of flow diversion isexciting. Bioresorbable flow diverters, using magnesium alloys or biodegradable polymers, aim to provide temporary flow diversion to scaffold aneurysm healing and then dissolve, eliminating the long-term presence of foreign material. The 2026 systematic review identified several platforms in development, though clinical data remain preliminary and no bioresorbable device has achieved regulatory approval. Drug-eluting surfaces, incorporating antiproliferative agents to accelerate endothelialization and reduce thrombosis risk, represent another investigational approach that may improve safety without compromising efficacy. AI-assisted sizing, using computational fluid dynamics modeling to predict flow diverter outcomes before implantation, may eventually personalize device selection for individual patients.

## Practical Implications

For the practicing neurointerventionalist, this evidence synthesis supports several actionable conclusions. Flow diversion is now the first-line treatment for large and giant wide-necked aneurysms of the internal carotid artery, with Level I evidence supporting its efficacy and safety. The treatment is also increasingly indicated for small and medium aneurysms, particularly in young patients where the durability advantage of flow diversion over coiling justifies the antiplatelet commitment. The decision between flow diversion and coiling should be individualized based on aneurysm morphology, patient age and comorbidities, and patient preference, with the durability advantage weighed against the antiplatelet requirement.

Antiplatelet preparation should be standardized and evidence-based. Either genotype-guided selection using CYP2C19 testing or universal use of potent P2Y12 inhibitors (ticagrelor or prasugrel) represents current best practice, replacing empiric clopidogrel in patients without documented clopidogrel responsiveness. Platelet function testing may identify patients who are inadequately inhibited despite apparently therapeutic DAPT. The specific antiplatelet regimen should be established in consultation with the patient's primary physician, particularly for patients who may require discontinuation of DAPT for upcoming procedures or surgeries.

Posterior circulation flow diversion should be approached with caution, particularly for basilar trunk aneurysms where the perforator infarction risk is highest. Selected cases at high-volume centers may achieve acceptable outcomes, but the risk-benefit calculation differs substantially from anterior circulation treatment. Patients should be counseled about the unique risks of posterior circulation flow diversion, including the possibility of brainstem infarction.

Follow-up should be planned proactively, with angiographic assessment at 6 months, 1 year, and extended intervals based on the extent of occlusion. Patients should understand that flow diversion is a progressive process and that early follow-up angiography may underestimate final occlusion rates.
