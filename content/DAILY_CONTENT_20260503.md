## SCRIPT

### Section 1: What Is a Flow Diverter? (0:30 - 2:00)
- A mesh tube stent placed inside the artery to divert blood flow away from aneurysm
- Unlike coiling (fills aneurysm from inside) or clipping (clamps base), flow diversion addresses the parent vessel wall
- Main devices: Pipeline Embolization Device (Medtronic), Surpass Evolve (Stryker), FRED (Terumo)
- Think of it like rerouting a river — you don't dam the lake, you redirect the current

### Section 2: The 5-Year Evidence (2:00 - 6:00)

**SCENT Trial — Surpass Flow Diverter**
- 5-year results published 2026
- Long-term safety: sustained, with no late complications emerging
- Effectiveness: aneurysm occlusion rates continued improving over 5 years — >85% complete occlusion
- Key insight: once the aneurysm heals, it stays healed. Late recurrence is rare.

**Pipeline FOREVER Trial (Internal Carotid Artery Aneurysms)**
- 5-year data: mRS 0-2 in 94%+ of patients at last follow-up
- Major complication rate: <3% — lower than surgical clipping for complex ICA aneurysms
- The caveat: these are centers with high volume operators. Results don't generalize as well to lower-volume centers.

**FRED Flow Diverter — 5-Year Efficacy**
- 5-year occlusion rates: ~80% complete occlusion
- Safety profile: consistent with other flow diverters
- Key paper: Stroke: Vascular and Interventional Neurology, 2026

**MCA M1 Aneurysms (Frontiers 2026)**
- Small series but important: 94.3% mRS 0-2 at median 13 months
- Shows flow diversion works even in challenging locations

### Section 3: Who Should Actually Get One? (6:00 - 8:00)
- **Best candidates:**
  - Large/giant aneurysms where coiling fails
  - Internal carotid artery aneurysms (best evidence base)
  - Fusiform aneurysms (no neck to clip or coil)
  - Patients with high surgical risk

- **When it fails:**
  - Very small parent vessels (<2mm) — harder to deploy
  - Aneurysms with incorporated branches — flow diverters can block side branches
  - When dual antiplatelet therapy is contraindicated

### Section 4: The Antiplatelet Problem (8:00 - 9:00)
- You need dual antiplatelet therapy (aspirin + clopidogrel) for at least 6 months
- For neurointerventionists: this is the biggest practical barrier
- Patients who can't tolerate DAPT: surgical clipping or coiling may still be better
- Newer devices exploring bioresorbable options — early data from 2026 JNIS paper on magnesium BRFD

### Section 5: Bottom Line (9:00 - 10:00)
1. Flow diverters have 5-year data proving long-term durability
2. Best for complex, large, or fusiform aneurysms in high-volume centers
3. The antiplatelet requirement is the main practical constraint
4. Outcomes depend heavily on operator experience
5. This is not a "better coiling" — it's a fundamentally different treatment paradigm

**CTA:** "If you or a family member has been told you need aneurysm treatment, ask your neurointerventionalist: am I a candidate for flow diversion? The long-term data is now strong enough to ask that question."

The data keeps expanding. But not every stroke patient benefits — and overtreating wastes resources, exposes patients to risk, and muddies the literature.

**Tweet 2:**
First, the easy wins:
• Anterior circulation LVO (ICA, M1)
• NIHSS ≥6 (moderate-severe deficit)
• ASPECTS ≥6 on CT
• Treatment within 6 hours (HERMES meta-analysis)

If you check these boxes, thrombectomy is clearly beneficial. That's ~15-20% of all ischemic strokes.

**Tweet 3:**
Now the interesting part: the LATE WINDOW.

DAWN & DEFUSE-3 showed benefit up to 24 hours.
But only in carefully selected patients:
→ Clinical mismatch (deficit too large for infarct size)
→ OR Target mismatch on perfusion (penumbra > infarct core)

NOT just "it's been 12 hours, let's try."

**Tweet 4:**
Here's the key insight most clinicians miss:

The 6-24 hour window is NOT for everyone. It's for patients where imaging shows the math works out.

Penumbra volume matters more than time itself.
A patient with small core + large penumbra at 20 hours > patient with large core at 4 hours.

**Tweet 5:**
Who DOESN'T benefit even with LVO?

• Large infarct core (ASPECTS <6, core >70mL on CTP/MRI)
• Bilateral watershed infarcts (poor collaterals)
• Fixed, prolonged deficits suggesting completed infarct
• M2 segment occlusions — modest benefit at best

**Tweet 6:**
New 2025-2026 data is refining selection even further:

• Posterior circulation (basilar): Evidence strengthening but still more controversial than anterior
• Extended age criteria: >90 years can still benefit if otherwise healthy
• Low NIHSS (≤5): CONTRAST trial showed no benefit in mild strokes — don't thrombectomize just because you can

Thrombectomy is one of neurology's greatest advances. But it's not a universal stroke treatment.

## JNIS (Journal of NeuroInterventional Surgery)

### 1. DWI-ADC Mismatch Predicts Infarct Growth Rate and EVT Outcomes
**Published:** 16 April 2026

**What it covers:** Diffusion-weighted imaging and ADC mapping to predict infarct growth rate and mechanical thrombectomy outcomes in anterior circulation stroke. DWI-ADC mismatch as imaging biomarker.

**Why it matters clinically:** Current thrombectomy selection relies heavily on time windows or perfusion mismatch. If validated, this could change how we image stroke patients before sending them to the angiosuite, especially in late-window cases.

## INR (Interventional Neuroradiology)

### 3. Flow Diverter Stents with Hydrophilic Polymer Coating for Acutely Ruptured Aneurysms — Single Antiplatelet Therapy
**Published:** 25 April 2026 (OnlineFirst)

**What it covers:** Preliminary experience using flow diverter stents with hydrophilic polymer coating for acutely ruptured aneurysms, with only single antiplatelet therapy (instead of dual antiplatelet).

**Why it matters clinically:** This is a significant practical advance. The main barrier to flow diversion in ruptured aneurysms is the need for dual antiplatelet therapy — particularly problematic when you need to place a stent in an acutely ruptured aneurysm. A hydrophilic polymer coating that allows single antiplatelet coverage could expand the use of flow diverters in the acute rupture setting. Early data only, but promising enough to watch closely.

### 5. Absence of FLAIR Hyperintensity — Therapeutic Window on MRI
**Published:** April 2026 (Vol 47, Issue 4)

**What it covers:** Study showing that absence of FLAIR hyperintensity on MRI suggests the stroke may still be within a therapeutic window — supporting use of MRI to guide treatment decisions.

**Why it matters clinically:** FLAIR imaging has been explored as a surrogate for tissue age in stroke. This supports using MRI — specifically FLAIR — as a decision tool rather than relying solely on time since last known well.
