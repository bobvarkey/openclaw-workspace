# X Thread: Thrombectomy — Who Actually Benefits?
**Tweet 1 of 8**
🧵 THREAD: Who should actually get mechanical thrombectomy?

The data keeps expanding. But not every stroke patient benefits — and overtreating wastes resources, exposes patients to risk, and muddies the literature.

Let me break down what the evidence actually says in 2026.

**Tweet 2**
First, the easy wins:
• Anterior circulation LVO (ICA, M1)
• NIHSS ≥6 (moderate-severe deficit)
• ASPECTS ≥6 on CT
• Treatment within 6 hours (HERMES meta-analysis)

If you check these boxes, thrombectomy is clearly beneficial. That's ~15-20% of all ischemic strokes.

**Tweet 3**
Now the interesting part: the LATE WINDOW.

DAWN & DEFUSE-3 showed benefit up to 24 hours.
But only in carefully selected patients:
→ Clinical mismatch (deficit too large for infarct size)
→ OR Target mismatch on perfusion (penumbra > infarct core)

NOT just "it's been 12 hours, let's try."

**Tweet 4**
Here's the key insight most clinicians miss:

The 6-24 hour window is NOT for everyone. It's for patients where imaging shows the math works out.

Penumbra volume matters more than time itself.
A patient with small core + large penumbra at 20 hours > patient with large core at 4 hours.

**Tweet 5**
Who DOESN'T benefit even with LVO?

• Large infarct core (ASPECTS <6, core >70mL on CTP/MRI)
• Bilateral watershed infarcts (poor collaterals)
• Fixed, prolonged deficits suggesting completed infarct
• M2 segment occlusions — modest benefit at best

**Tweet 6**
New 2025-2026 data is refining selection even further:

• Posterior circulation (basilar): Evidence strengthening but still more controversial than anterior
• Extended age criteria: >90 years can still benefit if otherwise healthy
• Low NIHSS (≤5): CONTRAST trial showed no benefit in mild strokes — don't thrombectomize just because you can

**Tweet 7**
The practical algorithm I use:

1. LVO on CTA? → Go to step 2
2. ASPECTS ≥6? → Go to step 3
3. <6 hours OR (6-24 hours AND mismatch profile)? → Thrombectomy
4. Otherwise → Medical management

Simple. Evidence-based. Reduces unnecessary procedures.

**Tweet 8**
Bottom line:

Thrombectomy is one of neurology's greatest advances. But it's not a universal stroke treatment.

The difference between a good outcome and a wasted procedure often comes down to one question: does the imaging show penumbra that's worth saving?

Ask your neurointerventionalist. Or find one who asks the right questions first.
