## Tweet 2

In ALS, detecting bulbar lower motor neuron involvement is critical for diagnosis and prognosis.

But here's the problem:

• SCM catches only ~42% of bulbar cases
• Genioglossus is most accurate but patients tolerate it poorly (gag reflex)
• Missing bulbar involvement = delayed diagnosis

---

## Tweet 3 (The Data)

Zhao et al. (2025) compared three muscles:

| Muscle | Abnormality Rate |
|--------|-----------------|
| **Trapezius** | **67%** ← best |
| SCM | 41.9% |
| Genioglossus | 40.4% |

Trapezius outperformed BOTH.

---

## Tweet 4 (Why It Works)

Trapezius = cranial nerve XI innervation (spinal accessory nerve)
- More reserve: larger muscle bulk, better tolerability
- Still correlates with bulbar LMN involvement
- Easier to needle, less discomfort for patients

If patient can't tolerate genioglossus → trapezius is your answer.

---

## Tweet 5 (Clinical Takeaway)

Best practice for ALS EMG when assessing bulbar involvement:

1. Trapezius — best sensitivity, best tolerated
2. SCM — reasonable backup, but ~23% miss rate
3. Genioglossus — most specific, use if patient tolerates

Simple algorithm: Start with trapezius. Add SCM. Only do genioglossus if you have to and patient can handle it.
