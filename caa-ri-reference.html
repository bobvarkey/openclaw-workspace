🏥 CAA-RI: Cerebral Amyloid Angiopathy-Related Inflammation

CAA-ri is a rare but treatable subset of cerebral amyloid angiopathy causing reversible encephalopathy with seizures, focal deficits, and inflammation. Often misdiagnosed — responds well to steroids.

🔬 Diagnostic Criteria (Chung et al.)

Probable CAA-ri = clinical features (subacute mental status change, headache, seizures, focal deficits) +:

  1. Radiological CAA (Boston criteria) — lobar microbleeds or hemorrhage
  2. Age ≥40 years
  3. Unifocal/multifocal white matter hyperintensity on T2/FLAIR MRI
  4. No alternate explanation
Validation: 82% sensitivity, 97% specificity

🧠 Imaging Findings

  • MRI: Patchy or confluent T2/FLAIR white matter hyperintensity — often asymmetric
  • Vasogenic edema in frontal/temporal lobes, subcortical white matter
  • Multifocal — 30% involve both hemispheres
  • SWI: Cerebral microbleeds at corticomedullary junction
  • Gadolinium enhancement — may see leptomeningeal/parenchymal enhancement (more common in ABRA)

💊 Treatment

First-line

High-dose corticosteroids: Dexamethasone 4mg TID × 2 weeks, then wean

Outcomes:
  • 60% recover without relapse
  • ~78% reduction in lesion size on follow-up MRI
  • Relapse risk: 40% at 1 year (if not on maintenance)
  • 75% reduced relapse with ongoing immunosuppression

💡 Key Takeaway

CAA-ri should be considered in elderly patients with new seizures, delirium, or focal deficits + MRI showing white matter changes + microbleeds. High-dose steroids lead to dramatic improvement — but condition is often missed.
