# The Book of Laughter and Forgetting — A Meditation on Memory, Power, and Two Kinds of Laughter

**Milan Kundera | 1979 | Translated from Czech by Aaron Asher**

---

## I. The Hat and the Erasure

The book opens on a balcony in Prague, February 1948. Klement Gottwald, the Communist leader of Czechoslovakia, stands before a crowd of hundreds of thousands. It's freezing. Vladimir Clementis, his comrade, takes off his own fur hat and places it on Gottwald's bare head. The propaganda photos capture the moment perfectly — Gottwald, the people's hero, in Clementis's hat. Four years later, Clementis is charged with treason and hanged. The propaganda department immediately airbrushes him out of every photograph. All that remains of Clementis now is **the hat on Gottwald's head**.

This is the central metaphor of the entire book: **power is the ability to make people forget.** History is not written by victors — it is *erased* by them. The erasure is not just political. It is personal. Intimate. Existential.

---

## II. Forgetting as a Way of Life

Kundera structures the book as seven separate but interlocking narratives — a "novel in the form of variations," he calls it — unified not by plot but by theme. Each part dances around the same haunting question: **What happens to a person (or a nation) when memory is systematically destroyed?**

There is **Mirek**, who in his youth wrote love letters to a woman during the Stalinist era. Decades later, he becomes obsessed with retrieving them — not out of love, but out of a desperate need to control his own narrative. He cannot bear the thought of his past being in someone else's hands. *"The struggle of man against power is the struggle of memory against forgetting."*

There is **Tamarina**, a woman who flees Czechoslovakia for a small island after her husband's political downfall. She loses her photographs in transit, and with them, her connection to her own past. She takes a job as a nanny for a couple who cannot have children, and in a strange, almost mythological turn, ends up swimming naked in a lake surrounded by the ghostly laughter of children she never had. She is literally being erased — becoming a ghost in her own life.

There is **Jan and his wife**, whose marriage is so sanitized, so polite, that they cannot speak a single honest word to each other. Their love has been forgotten by them.

And there is **Kundera himself**, stepping onto the page in the first person, reflecting on his own childhood, his father's death, the sound of Beethoven quarters, and the strange business of writing a book whose very existence is an act of defiance against forgetting.

---

## III. Two Laughter of Laughter

One of the most brilliant moves in the book — and the one that gives it its title — is Kundera's taxonomy of laughter.

**The Devil's Laughter:** When we see something sacred, something grand, and suddenly realize it's absurd — we laugh. This is the laughter of things *losing their meaning*. It is subversive, liberating, destructive. The Devil laughs because he sees the emptiness behind the mask.

**The Angel's Laughter:** But the Angel, too, laughs. The Angel's laughter is the opposite — it is the laughter of *too much meaning*. Of the faithful who are so certain, so ecstatically joyful in their belief, that their laughter rings with the sound of total certainty. The Angel's laughter is the sound of ideology, of dogma, of the crowd that has stopped questioning.

Kundera's insight is devastating: **both are dangerous.** The Devil's laughter leaves you with nothing. The Angel's laughter gives you everything — which is a different kind of nothing. The novel exists in the space between them, in the uneasy tension where a person can laugh *at* something without becoming either a cynic or a fanatic.

## IV. Litost

There is a Czech word that Kundera insists cannot be properly translated: *litost*.

He defines it as *"a state of torment created by the sudden sight of one's own misery."* It is not quite self-pity. It is not quite regret. It is the visceral, almost physical shock of seeing yourself as small, pathetic, forgotten — and the violent, desperate urge to strike back at the world that made you feel that way.

He illustrates it through a story about a young man whose girlfriend humiliates him by dancing with another man. He goes home and stares at himself in the mirror, consumed by a rage he cannot name. Years later, he will still feel it. He will never quite get over it. *Litost* is the emotional residue of being forgotten — even for a moment.

And on a national level, *litost* is what happens to a country that has been airbrushed out of history. The Czechs, Kundera suggests, have been living with *litost* for centuries — a small nation squeezed between empires, erased from maps, forgotten by the world.

---

## V. The Circle Dance

There is a recurring image in the book: people holding hands and dancing in a circle. At first, it seems innocent — a folk dance, a celebration. But Kundera returns to it again and again, and gradually its meaning shifts. The circle dance becomes the symbol of totalitarian joy — the forced happiness of people who have surrendered their individuality to the group. They hold hands, they laugh, they spin. They have forgotten how to be alone.

It is, I think, the most terrifying image in the book. Not the gulags. Not the executions. Just people dancing in a circle, laughing, because they have been told to laugh.

---

## VI. Form and Fragmentation

Structurally, the book is extraordinary. It jumps between fiction and essay, between third-person narrative and first-person confession. Kundera includes a dream sequence, a meditation on the history of the waltz, an analysis of his own compositional choices, and a long passage about his father composing a Beethoven symphony from memory as he goes deaf.

This fragmentation is not a failure of form — it *is* the form. Forgetting is fragmentation. Memory is not a straight line; it is a shattered mirror. Kundera's novel mirrors what it describes. You cannot write a coherent, well-ordered novel about forgetting. The form must break apart to be honest.

---

## VII. Why It Matters

*The Book of Laughter and Forgetting* was written in 1979, but it has only become more urgent. We live in an age of unprecedented erasure — not just of history, but of attention, of nuance, of the ability to hold two contradictory thoughts at once. Social media scrolls are the new propaganda airbrush. Algorithmic feeds erase context. The circle dance has gone digital.

Kundera's book is a warning: **remember, or be erased.** Not because anyone is coming to kill you (though they might), but because forgetting is a slow death. It is the death of the self, scattered across days and years, until you look in the mirror and see nothing but a hat on someone else's head.

The last line of the book: *"The novelist teaches the reader to comprehend the world as a question."* There is no answer. There is only the act of remembering to ask.
