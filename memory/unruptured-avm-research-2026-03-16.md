# Unruptured Cerebral AVM Treatments — Research Report
**Date:** 2026-03-16
**Compiled by:** Dwight Neurovascular Research Intel

---

## Executive Summary

The management of unruptured brain arteriovenous malformations (AVMs) remains one of the most contentious topics in neurovascular surgery. The landmark ARUBA trial (2014, final follow-up 2020) demonstrated superiority of medical management over interventional therapy for preventing death or symptomatic stroke over a mean 50-month follow-up. However, significant criticism has emerged regarding ARUBA's methodology — particularly the low proportion of microsurgical patients (only 18%), short follow-up, and high crossover rates. Subsequent real-world data from registries (MATCH, NVQI-QOD, TOBAS, UCSF) and propensity-matched analyses have challenged ARUBA's blanket recommendation, showing that microsurgical resection of low-grade (SM I-II) unruptured AVMs achieves near-complete obliteration (97-100%) with acceptable complication rates. The MATCH registry (2025) — the largest prospective study to date with 1,770 patients — demonstrated that interventional treatment was associated with significantly higher hemorrhage-free survival at both 5 years (96% vs 89%) and 10 years (84% vs 76%). Current consensus favors individualized, grade-stratified decision-making with multidisciplinary team evaluation.

## 1. Key RCTs and Trials

### 1.1 ARUBA Trial (A Randomized Trial of Unruptured Brain AVMs)

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Study Design** | Multicentre, non-blinded, RCT |
| **Registration** | NCT00389181 |
| **Sites** | 39 centres, 9 countries |
| **Enrollment** | April 2007 – April 2013 (stopped early by DSMB) |
| **Screened** | 1,740 patients |
| **Randomized** | 226 patients (110 medical management, 116 interventional therapy) |
| **Mean Follow-up** | 33.3 months (initial), 50.4 months (final) |

**Intervention Arms:**
- Medical management alone (pharmacological therapy for symptoms)
- Medical management + interventional therapy (surgery 18%, embolization 32%, radiosurgery 33%, embolization + radiosurgery 16%)

**Key Results (Final Follow-up, Lancet Neurology 2020):**
- Death or symptomatic stroke: **10.1%** medical management vs **30.7%** interventional therapy (initial analysis)
- Extended follow-up: **15/110** (3.39/100 patient-years) medical vs **41/116** (12.32/100 patient-years) interventional
- **HR 0.31** (95% CI 0.17–0.56) favoring medical management
- Deaths: 2 in medical arm, 4 in interventional arm (2 attributed to intervention)
- Adverse events: 283 vs 369 (58.97 vs 78.73 per 100 patient-years)
- Median mRS at final follow-up significantly better in medical management group

**Key Criticisms:**
1. Only 18/114 interventional patients had microsurgery (18%) — vastly underrepresents the gold standard
2. Short follow-up (33 months initial) inadequate to capture long-term obliteration benefit
3. High proportion of partially treated AVMs (embolization-only, incomplete SRS)
4. 13% screening-to-randomization rate (226/1,740) — severe selection bias
5. Many high-volume centres refused to randomize low-grade AVMs
6. No stratification by Spetzler-Martin grade in primary analysis
7. High crossover rate (7 medical→interventional; 20 interventional→medical)

**PMIDs:**
- Initial results: **24268105** (Lancet 2014;383:614-21)
- Final follow-up: **32562682** (Lancet Neurol 2020;19:573-81)

### 1.2 TOBAS Trial (Treatment of Brain AVMs)

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Study Design** | Pragmatic RCT with prospective observation registry |
| **Registration** | NCT02098252 |
| **Population** | 434 patients (87% unruptured) |
| **Mean Follow-up** | 3.3 years (>1,300 patient-years) |
| **Grading** | Roughly even split low-grade vs high-grade (SM) |

**Key Results (ESMINT 2024 Interim Analysis):**
- Primary endpoint (mRS >2 or worsening by ≥1 point if baseline mRS >2): **23/434 (5.3%)** reached endpoint
- Incidence of death or new/worsened dependency: **1.7 per 100 patient-years** (all patients)
- For **low-grade unruptured AVMs specifically**: **0.6 per 100 patient-years** (95% CI 0.2–1.7)
- New ICH: 38/434 (9%), overall incidence **2.8 per 100 patient-years**
- Low-grade unruptured major ICH incidence: **1.3 per 100 patient-years**
- Major AVM rupture in low-grade unruptured: 4%, with 0.6% fatal
- Serious adverse events (all): **3.6 per 100 patient-years**
- SAEs for low-grade unruptured: **1.8 per 100 patient-years**
- 26 patients (6%) crossed over to treatment during follow-up

**Key Conclusions:**
- Observation of low-grade unruptured AVMs carries relatively low event rates
- Risk factors for poor outcomes: previous rupture, high-grade, infratentorial location, deep venous drainage, age ≥55 years
- Results "in line with previous studies" identifying rupture status, deep venous drainage, and infratentorial location as key risk factors

### 1.3 UCSF ARUBA-Eligible Cohort

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Study Design** | Single-centre, prospective registry |
| **Population** | 74 ARUBA-eligible patients |
| **Treated** | 61 patients (70.5% had surgical resection ± preop embolization) |
| **Observed** | 13 patients |

**Key Results:**
- 1 observed patient died from AVM hemorrhage (1/13)
- 9 treated patients had stroke or died (9/61)
- **No significant difference** in stroke/death rate: HR 1.34 (95% CI 0.12–14.53, p=0.807)
- No significant difference in clinical impairment (mRS ≥2)
- Treatment outcomes **better than ARUBA** intervention arm
- Only 4/473 screened patients enrolled in ARUBA (clinicians preferred not to randomize)

**Conclusion:** Results in ARUBA-eligible patients managed with surgery at a high-volume centre lead to "an entirely different conclusion" due to primary role of surgery, judicious selection, and technical expertise.

**PMID:** PMC4420142 (Neurosurg Focus 2014;37(3):E8)

### 1.4 MATCH Registry (China)

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Study Design** | Nationwide, multicenter prospective cohort with target trial emulation |
| **Population** | 1,770 unruptured AVM patients |
| **Period** | August 2011 – December 2021 |
| **Median Age** | 26.2 years (IQR 16.5–37.6) |
| **Male** | 59.8% |

**Key Results (JAMA Network Open, 2025):**

| Outcome | Interventional | Conservative | Difference |
|---------|---------------|-------------|------------|
| 5-year hemorrhage-free survival | **96.23%** (95% CI 93.95–97.65) | **89.00%** (95% CI 86.37–91.24) | 7.23% (95% CI 4.78–9.91) |
| 10-year hemorrhage-free survival | **84%** | **76%** | 8.37% (95% CI 2.68–15.70) |
| HR for hemorrhage (5-year) | — | — | **0.44** (95% CI 0.33–0.57) |
| HR for hemorrhage (10-year) | — | — | **0.56** (95% CI 0.42–0.69) |

**Critical Finding:** The benefit of intervention was **not observed** in patients with high-grade AVMs or diffuse nidus — outcomes were similar to conservative management. Only **microsurgical resection** was consistently associated with reduced hemorrhage risk in sensitivity analyses.

**PMID:** 41231469

## 2. Treatment Modalities Comparison

### 2.1 Microsurgical Resection

**Strengths:**
- Highest obliteration rates: **97.8%** for SM I-II (MISTA 2025), **87.1%** for SM IV (MISTA 2025)
- **Immediate** protection from hemorrhage upon successful resection
- Gold standard for low-grade (SM I-II) AVMs
- Long-term cure if angiographic complete resection confirmed

**Risks:**
- Perioperative hemorrhage, infection, new neurological deficits
- Complication rates: ~11% symptomatic, ~6-7% permanent for SM I-II
- Higher morbidity for SM IV-V (32.3% complication rate)
- Requires experienced high-volume centre

**Key Evidence (MISTA 2025, Neurosurgical Review):**
- SM I-II: Resection 97.8% obliteration vs SRS 60.0% (p<0.001)
- Unruptured SM I-II: Resection 100% obliteration vs SRS 58.3% (p<0.001)
- Symptomatic complications: 11.1% each (resection vs SRS)
- Permanent complications: 6.7% resection vs 5.6% SRS (p=0.8)
- Functional improvement rates similar: 67.2% resection vs 66.7% SRS

### 2.2 Stereotactic Radiosurgery (SRS)

**Strengths:**
- Non-invasive, outpatient procedure
- Suitable for deep-seated or eloquent AVMs
- Lower immediate procedural risk
- Good option for surgically inaccessible lesions

**Risks:**
- **Latency period** (2-5 years) during which hemorrhage risk persists
- Obliteration rates vary by grade: 60-80% for SM I-III, **32.3% for SM IV** (MISTA 2025)
- Adverse radiation effects (AREs): ~9% overall
- Radiation edema, necrosis
- May require repeat treatment for residual nidus

**Key Evidence:**

**Pre-ARUBA GKRS series (Nature Sci Rep, 2020):**
- N=173 unruptured AVMs, median SM grade 2
- Obliteration rate: **76%** at 12 years (first GKRS)
- With second GKRS: **100%** obliteration
- Median time to obliteration: 4.3 years
- Hemorrhage rate: 10% (all within first 4 years), annual risk 1.1%
- mRS ≤1 at last follow-up: 86%
- Epilepsy cure: 84.6% seizure-free

**High-grade SRS meta-analysis (J Neurosurg, 2025):**
- N=562 high-grade AVMs (SM IV-V), 15 studies
- Overall obliteration: **34.2%** (95% CI 27.0–42.1%, median follow-up 50 months)
- Post-SRS hemorrhage: 12.2% (95% CI 7.8–18.7%)
- Previously ruptured: 12.7% hemorrhage vs unruptured: 5.2% hemorrhage
- AREs incidence: **9.3%** (95% CI 5.2–15.9%)

**PMID:** 39919274

### 2.3 Endovascular Embolization

**Role:**
- Primarily **adjunctive** — rarely curative as standalone
- ARUBA subgroup analysis (AJNR 2020) examined embolization-only outcomes
- Pre-surgical devascularization to reduce nidus size
- Targeting of high-risk features (flow-related aneurysms, intranidal aneurysms)
- Potential pre-SRS volume reduction

**Risks:**
- Stroke, vessel injury, incomplete obliteration
- In ARUBA, embolization-only (32% of intervention arm) had high complication rates
- NVQI-QOD real-world data: compared with ARUBA trial outcomes

**Evidence:**
- ARUBA subgroup: Embolization-only outcomes worse than expected from single-centre series
- Standalone cure rates: typically 10-40% depending on AVM angioarchitecture
- Best utilized as part of multimodal approach

## 3. Grade-Specific Recommendations

### SM Grade I-II (Low Grade)

| Factor | Recommendation |
|--------|---------------|
| **Preferred Treatment** | **Microsurgical resection** — gold standard |
| **Rationale** | Near-100% obliteration, low morbidity in experienced hands |
| **SRS Alternative** | For eloquent/surgically inaccessible lesions (60% obliteration) |
| **Observation** | Reasonable for small, incidental lesions in older patients; event rate ~0.6-1.3/100 pt-years |
| **Key Evidence** | MISTA 2025: Resection 97.8% vs SRS 60% obliteration (p<0.001) |

### SM Grade III (Intermediate)

| Factor | Recommendation |
|--------|---------------|
| **Preferred Treatment** | **Multimodal approach** — individualized decision |
| **Options** | Resection (if accessible), SRS (if deep/eloquent), staged embolization + SRS |
| **Observation** | Higher risk than low-grade; discuss with multidisciplinary team |
| **Key Evidence** | No single RCT; observational data supports case-by-case approach |

### SM Grade IV-V (High Grade)

| Factor | Recommendation |
|--------|---------------|
| **Preferred Treatment** | **Observation** favored for unruptured; intervention only for carefully selected cases |
| **SRS** | Modest obliteration (32-34%), higher ARE risk (9%), long latency |
| **Resection** | Higher obliteration (87%) but significant morbidity (32% complications) |
| **Multimodal** | Embolization + resection or embolization + SRS for select cases |
| **Key Evidence** | MISTA 2025 SM IV: Resection 87.1% vs SRS 32.3% obliteration (p<0.001); JNS 2025 meta-analysis: SRS obliteration 34.2% |
| **MATCH 2025** | Intervention benefit NOT observed in high-grade or diffuse AVMs |

## 4. Risk Factors Favoring Treatment vs Observation

### Favor Treatment:
- Young patient age (longer lifetime hemorrhage risk)
- SM Grade I-II
- Superficial location, non-eloquent
- Progressive symptoms (worsening epilepsy, deficits)
- Associated intranidal aneurysm
- Deep venous drainage (higher hemorrhage risk)
- Patient preference after informed consent

### Favor Observation:
- SM Grade IV-V
- Deep/eloquent location
- Diffuse nidus
- Older age (>55 years)
- Asymptomatic/Incidental
- Patient preference

### Natural History Risk Factors for Hemorrhage:
- Previous rupture (strongest predictor)
- Deep venous drainage
- Deep brain location
- Increasing age
- Associated aneurysms

## 5. Impact of ARUBA on Clinical Practice

**Patel et al. (2022), National Inpatient Sample analysis:**
- PMID: 36272182
- NIS 2009-2018: 88,037 projected AVM admissions
- 82.7% were unruptured AVMs
- Interventional treatment rates declined from 35.4% (2012) to 26.4% (2018)
- Driven by reduction in endovascular treatment (18.8% → 13.9%) and inpatient SRS (0.5% → 0.1%)
- Corresponding increase in ruptured AVM admissions: 17.0% → 23.3%
- Increased odds of discharge to long-term facility or death post-ARUBA (OR 1.14, p=0.02)

**Interpretation:** ARUBA shifted practice toward watchful waiting, which correlated with increased ruptured AVM admissions — an expected consequence of deferred treatment.

## 6. Current Consensus (2025-2026)

1. **ARUBA alone should not drive treatment decisions.** Its methodology limitations (low surgery rate, short follow-up, poor generalizability) are widely acknowledged.

2. **Grade-stratified approach is essential.** SM Grade I-II unruptured AVMs in experienced hands have excellent outcomes with microsurgery.

3. **MATCH registry (2025) is the most robust evidence** to date favoring intervention for hemorrhage prevention, particularly with microsurgical resection.

4. **Multidisciplinary team evaluation** (neurosurgeon, neurointerventionalist, radiation oncologist, neurologist) is mandatory.

5. **Patient-centered shared decision-making** incorporating natural history risk, treatment risks, patient age, and AVM angioarchitecture.

6. **High-grade unruptured AVMs** generally favor observation unless specific risk factors warrant intervention.

7. **SRS is a reasonable alternative** for SM I-III AVMs that are not ideal surgical candidates, with acknowledgment of the latency period risk.

8. **Embolization alone is rarely curative** and is best used as adjunct to surgery or SRS.

## 8. Summary of Key Findings

| Domain | Key Finding |
|--------|-------------|
| **ARUBA** | Medical management superior at 50 months (HR 0.31), but low surgery rate (18%) limits applicability |
