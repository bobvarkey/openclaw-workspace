# Thoracic Radiculopathy (Radiculoneuropathy)

## Overview

Thoracic radiculopathy is a rare form of radiculopathy affecting the upper/mid-spine (T1-T12). It occurs when a nerve root in the thoracic region becomes compressed or irritated, causing pain and neurological symptoms along the nerve's distribution.

## Epidemiology

- Relatively rare (much less common than cervical or lumbar radiculopathy)
- Incidence: ~1 in 1,000 population
- Asymptomatic thoracic disc herniations: up to 37%
- Most common in 4th-6th decade
- Male:female ratio ~3:2

## Causes

### Common Causes
1. **Herniated Disc** (most common in patients <50)
   - 95% of disc herniations occur at L4/5 or L5/S1 (lumbar)
   - Thoracic disc herniation much less common

2. **Degenerative Changes** (most common in patients >50)
   - Spinal stenosis
   - Facet joint osteoarthritis
   - Osteophyte formation

3. **Foraminal Stenosis**
   - Narrowing of the intervertebral foramen
   - Compression of exiting nerve root

4. **Other Causes**
   - Trauma/fracture
   - Scheuermann's disease
   - Tumor (rare)
   - Infection (discitis, osteomyelitis)

## Clinical Presentation

### Symptoms
- **Radicular pain**: Sharp, burning, or shooting pain along nerve distribution
- **Dermatomal sensory changes**: Numbness, tingling, paresthesia
- **Myotomal weakness**: Muscle weakness in affected level
- **Gait abnormalities** (if severe)

### Pattern
- Pain follows predictable patterns based on affected nerve root
- May present as chest wall or abdominal pain (can mimic visceral pathology)
- Pain often worsens with certain movements or positions

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Assessment
- Detailed neurological examination
- Motor and sensory testing by dermatome/myotome
- Reflex assessment
- Provocative tests

### Diagnostic Tools
1. **Imaging**
   - MRI: Gold standard for soft tissue evaluation
   - CT: For bony detail, especially if surgery considered
   - X-ray: Initial screening, rules out fractures

2. **Electrodiagnostic Studies**
   - EMG (Electromyography)
   - Nerve conduction studies (NCS)
   - Helps confirm diagnosis and localize level

## Treatment

### Conservative (First-line)
- **Physical therapy**: Posture correction, flexibility, strengthening
- **Activity modification**: Avoid exacerbating movements
- **NSAIDs**: Ibuprofen, naproxen for pain/inflammation
- **Neuropathic pain medications**: Gabapentin, pregabalin

### Interventional
- **Epidural Steroid Injections (ESI)**
  - Thoracic epidural steroid injection (TESI)
  - Target: reduce inflammation around nerve root
  - Evidence supports efficacy for radicular pain
  - Fluoroscopy-guided for accuracy

- **Selective nerve root block**

### Surgical (Refractory cases)
- **Discectomy**: For herniated disc
- **Laminectomy/laminoplasty**: For stenosis
- **Foraminotomy**: For foraminal stenosis
- **Spinal fusion**: If instability present

## Prognosis

- Majority of cases are benign and resolve spontaneously
- Conservative management effective in most patients
- Some patients may progress to sensory deficits and motor weakness
- Early intervention improves outcomes

## Clinical Pearls

1. Thoracic radiculopathy is often misdiagnosed as cardiac, pulmonary, or abdominal pathology
2. Most disc herniations in thoracic spine are asymptomatic
3. Overtreatment should be avoided - many cases resolve without intervention
4. MRI findings must correlate with clinical symptoms
5. Consider red flags: weight loss, night pain, neurological deficits - rule out malignancy
