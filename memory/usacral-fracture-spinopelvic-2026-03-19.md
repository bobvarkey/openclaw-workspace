# U-Shaped Sacral Fracture & Spinopelvic Dissociation: Comprehensive Review

**Date:** March 19, 2026  
**Author:** Dwight (Neuroscience Research Agent)  
**Topic:** U-shaped sacral fractures, H-type sacral fractures, spinopelvic dissociation

---

## Executive Summary

U-shaped and H-type sacral fractures represent a rare but clinically significant subset of sacral injuries, comprising approximately 4-5% of all sacral fractures. These injuries are characterized by spinopelvic dissociation (SPD), where the sacral fracture disrupts force transfer from the spine to the pelvis. Key findings from this review:

- **Epidemiology:** Bimodal distribution - high-energy trauma in young patients (MVCs, falls from height) vs. low-energy fragility fractures in elderly with osteoporosis
- **Neurological deficits:** Present in up to 94% of cases; sacral nerve roots, cauda equina commonly affected
- **Classification systems:** Roy-Camille (3 types based on displacement), Denis (zones I-III), AOSpine classification
- **Treatment:** Surgical management preferred for U/H-type fractures; lumbopelvic fixation (LPF) and iliosacral screw fixation (ISF) are primary techniques
- **Outcomes:** 90% excellent/good functional outcomes with spinopelvic fixation; 73% neurological improvement
- **Complications:** Hardware prominence (21.3%), infections (7.2%), wound complications

## 1. Classification Systems

### 1.1 Denis Classification (1988)
Based on relationship to sacral foramina and sacral canal:
- **Zone I:** Fractures through sacral ala (lateral to foramina)
- **Zone II:** Fractures through sacral foramina (may affect nerve roots)
- **Zone III:** Fractures through sacral canal (central, highest neurological risk)

### 1.2 Roy-Camille Classification
Based on flexion/extension angulation and translation direction:
- **Type 1:** Flexion angulation with anterior translation ("opening" fracture)
- **Type 2:** Flexion angulation with posterior translation
- **Type 3:** Extension angulation with anterior translation

Higher Roy-Camille classification correlates with increased neurological injury risk.

### 1.3 Morphological Classification
- **U-shaped:** Transverse fracture through sacrum with bilateral longitudinal extensions
- **H-shaped:** Bilateral longitudinal fractures with transverse component
- **T-shaped:** Unilateral longitudinal with transverse extension
- **Lambda-type:** Similar to T but incomplete

### 1.4 AOSpine Sacral Fracture Classification
Modern classification incorporating:
- Morphology (A: transverse, B: longitudinal, C: combined)
- Neurological status
- Patient-specific modifiers

## 2. Biomechanics and Mechanism of Injury

### 2.1 Mechanism
- **High-energy:** Axial loading from falls from height, MVCs ("suicide jumper's fracture")
- **Low-energy:** Ground-level falls in osteoporotic patients (fragility fractures)
- **Iatrogenic:** Sacral fractures following long-segment lumbosacral fusion

### 2.2 Biomechanics of Spinopelvic Dissociation
- Disruption of load transfer from lumbar spine to pelvis
- Vertical shear forces through sacrum
- Sacrum acts as "keystone" - loss of integrity compromises pelvic ring
- Neurological compromise from:
  - Direct nerve root compression
  - Cauda equina entrapment
  - Progressive kyphotic deformity

## 3. Surgical vs. Conservative Management

### 3.1 Indications for Surgery
- **Absolute:** Spinopelvic dissociation, neurological deficit, progressive deformity
- **Relative:** Displacement >1cm, bilateral fractures, associated pelvic ring injury

### 3.2 Conservative Management
- Reserved for:
  - Non-displaced fractures
  - Insufficient medical fitness for surgery
  - Isolated sacral ala fractures (Zone I)
- Treatment: Analgesia, physiotherapy-assisted mobilization
- Poor outcomes if untreated: chronic pain, deformity, neurological deterioration

### 3.3 Key Evidence

| Study | Design | N | Key Findings |
|-------|--------|---|--------------|
| Gribnau et al. (2009) | Case series | 41 | Surgical treatment improves outcomes; QOL significantly better than conservative |

## 4. Instrumentation Techniques

### 4.1 Iliosacral (IS) Screw Fixation
- **Technique:** Percutaneous placement of 7-8mm cannulated screws across SI joint
- **Advantages:** Minimally invasive, low blood loss, fast recovery
- **Limitations:** May not provide adequate stability for severe SPD
- **Complications:** Screw malposition, nerve root injury, implant loosening (14-20% in osteoporotic bone)

### 4.2 Lumbopelvic Fixation (LPF)
- **Technique:** Pedicle screws at L4-L5, bilateral iliac screws, connected by rods
- **Indications:** Severe SPD, bilateral sacral fractures, failed IS fixation
- **Advantages:** Excellent biomechanical stability, allows early mobilization
- **Disadvantages:** Invasive, higher blood loss, potential for infection
- Often combined with sacral decompression for neurological symptoms

### 4.3 Triangular Osteosynthesis
- **Technique:** Combination of LPF + iliosacral screws
- **Biomechanics:** Most stable construct per cadaveric studies
- **Applications:** Vertically unstable pelvic fractures, severe SPD

### 4.4 Transsacral Bar (TSB) Osteosynthesis
- **Technique:** Horizontal bar through S1 body, stabilized with washers and nuts
- **Advantages:**
  - Lower implant loosening rates vs IS screws (biomechanically stronger)
  - Transfers load through cortical bone of posterior ilium
  - Suitable for bilateral sacral fractures
- **Limitations:** Safe corridor absent in ~50% of patients (dysmorphic sacrum)
- **Recent Data (2021):** 85 patients, low mortality, high mobility restoration
- **For H-type fractures:** Can be combined with iliosacral screw for rotational stability

### 4.5 S2-Alar-Iliac (S2AI) Screw
- **Technique:** Starting at S2, directed through ala into ilium
- **Advantages:** Lower profile than traditional iliac screws, less hardware prominence
- **Biomechanical studies:** Comparable stability to iliac screws

### 4.6 Minimally Invasive Techniques
- Percutaneous fixation with navigation/3D guidance
- Reduces blood loss, wound complications
- Comparable outcomes to open surgery
- Recent advances: 3D-navigated transsacral bar osteosynthesis

## 5. Outcomes and Complications

### 5.1 Functional Outcomes

**Key Study: European Spine Journal (2019) - 41 patients**
- Mean follow-up: 4.1 years
- Pain: 40/41 patients reported pain at 1 year, no change at 2 years
- Neurological deficits: 36/39 had lower extremity deficits at baseline; improved at 1 year (p=0.0002)
- Bowel/bladder dysfunction: Common, urinary function deteriorated over time
- SF-36: Patient-reported health significantly worse than normal population

**Kelly et al. (Journal of Spine Surgery) - 16 patients**
- ISF vs LPF comparison
- LPF: Higher operative time (326 vs 89 min, p=0.0017), lower EBL
- Neurological improvement: Majority improved post-operatively
- Discharge disposition: ISF → skilled nursing; LPF → home (p=0.04)

### 5.2 Systematic Review Outcomes (2022)
- **Functional outcomes:** Excellent/good in 90% (95% CI 84-95%)
- **Radiological outcomes:** Excellent/good in 97% (95% CI 94-100%)
- **Neurological recovery:** 73% partial or complete improvement

### 5.3 Complications

| Complication | Rate | Notes |
|--------------|------|-------|
| Hardware prominence | 21.3% | Most common; may require removal |
| Infections | 7.2% | Wound complications significant concern |
| Implant loosening | 14-20% | Higher in osteoporotic bone |
| Neurological deterioration | Variable | Usually from screw malposition |
| Nonunion | 5-10% | Risk with conservative treatment |

### 5.4 Recent Studies 2024-2025

**Lumbopelvic Fixation Multicenter Study (2024)**
- Focus: Union rates, complications, neurological outcomes
- Preliminary findings: High union rates with LPF

**Percutaneous vs Open Fixation (2025)**
- Comparable outcomes
- Percutaneous: Lower wound complications, shorter LOS
- Open: Better fracture reduction capability

## 7. Clinical Recommendations

### 7.1 Algorithm for Management

1. **Diagnosis:** CT scan with sagittal/coronal reconstructions
2. **Assess:** Neurological status, displacement, SPD presence
3. **Treatment Selection:**
   - Non-displaced, no SPD → Conservative
   - Displaced or SPD → Surgical
   - Neurological deficit → LPF + decompression
   - Fragility fracture → Consider TSB or augmented IS screws
   - Severe SPD → Triangular osteosynthesis

### 7.2 Follow-up
- CT at 3-6 months to assess union
- Monitor neurological recovery
- Consider hardware removal after union (especially if prominent)
