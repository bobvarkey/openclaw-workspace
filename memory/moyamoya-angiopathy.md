# Moyamoya Angiopathy - INSC 2026 Lecture Notes

## Overview

Moyamoya angiopathy (MMA) is a chronic progressive occlusion of bilateral internal carotid arteries. Collateral circulation develops through Moyamoya vessels, periventricular collaterals, ECA collaterals, and PCA supply.

---

## Unstable Moyamoya Angiopathy

### Concept
Unstable Moyamoya = phase where cerebral perfusion is **insufficient** despite developing collaterals → frequent ischemic events (TIAs/strokes).

**Key characteristics:**
- Frequent TIAs
- Rapid angiographic progression
- Insufficient collateral circulation
- Hemodynamic compromise
- Increased ischemic stroke risk

---

## Case Examples

### Unstable Moyamoya A
- 30-year-old female, bilateral MMA
- Left Suzuki stage 4, Right Suzuki stage 1
- Left combined revascularization
- Recurrent right hemispheric TIA
- **Management:** Defer surgery, maintain hydration, MR perfusion

### Unstable Moyamoya B
- 30-year-old female, frequent drop attacks
- Bilateral Suzuki stage 3
- Dense Moyamoya collaterals, ICA narrowing
- **Collateral status determines outcome:**
  - Critically low → Type B unstable
  - Low → Ischemic symptoms
  - Sufficient → Asymptomatic
  - Exuberant → Hemorrhagic risk

### Unstable Moyamoya C
- 40-year-old female
- Bilateral Suzuki stage 5
- 4-5 TIAs daily
- Severe ICA occlusion, ECA collaterals, posterior compensation

---

## Hemodynamic Stroke in Moyamoya

**Critical concept:** Moyamoya stroke is primarily **hemodynamic** (hypoperfusion-driven), NOT thrombotic or embolic. This significantly influences treatment strategy.

---

## Hydration vs Antiplatelet Therapy

**Hemodynamic principle:** Pipeline stenosis → increased flow velocity → decreased distal pressure → if stenosis can't be corrected, increase upstream pressure.

**Clinical implication:** Adequate hydration improves cerebral perfusion.

**Recommendations:**
- Maintain good hydration
- Avoid dehydration  
- Maintain hydration especially perioperatively

---

## Hemorrhagic Moyamoya

Occurs due to fragile collateral vessels and microaneurysms.

| Type | Finding | Management |
|------|---------|------------|
| Type 1 | Periventricular collaterals (Suzuki 4) | Surgical revascularization |
| Type 2 | Microaneurysms in collaterals (Suzuki 3) | Surgical revascularization |

---

## Suzuki Stage Limitations

Suzuki staging alone does **NOT** determine:
- Symptom severity
- Ischemic vs hemorrhagic presentation
- Need for revascularization

**Clinical decisions require:** perfusion imaging + symptom profile + hemodynamic assessment

---

## Hemodynamic / Vascular Factors

Key biomechanical contributors:
1. Arterial tortuosity
2. Vascular branching angles
3. Wall shear stress
4. Blood vessel wall strength
5. Blood viscosity/rheology
6. Molecular factors

---

## Collateral Circulation Outcomes

| Collateral State | Clinical Outcome |
|------------------|------------------|
| Sufficient | Asymptomatic |
| Insufficient | Ischemic symptoms |
| Exuberant | Hemorrhagic complications |

---

## Final Conclusions

- Moyamoya = progressive ICA occlusion
- Collaterals attempt to maintain perfusion
- Ischemic + hemorrhagic variants = same disease spectrum
- **Outcome depends on collateral adequacy**
