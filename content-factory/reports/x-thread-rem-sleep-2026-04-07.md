## Tweet 2/8
During REM (Rapid Eye Movement) sleep:
- Hippocampus replays the day's events
- Neocortex integrates them with existing memories
- Emotional content gets "untangled" from factual memory
- Novel connections form between unrelated concepts

This is why you sometimes wake up with the answer to a problem you couldn't solve yesterday.

## Tweet 3/8
🧪 The evidence:
- Walker & Born (Nature, 2007): Learning-dependent spindles during NREM predict next-day performance gains
- Stickgold (J Neuroscience, 2005): REM selectively enhances hippocampal-neocortical memory transfer
- Ellenbogen et al.: NREM sleep spindles correlate with procedural memory consolidation

Sleep isn't rest.
It's active cognitive consolidation.

## Tweet 4/8
🩺 Clinical correlations I see in practice:

1. Sleep deprivation → impaired procedural learning (muscle memory, procedural skills)
2. REM disruption → emotional dysregulation, anxiety amplification
3. REM behavior disorder → α-synuclein pathology (Parkinson's, DLB) — early marker
4. Narcolepsy with cataplexy → orexin/hypocretin loss → fragmented REM sleep architecture

Your sleep study tells a neurological story.

## Tweet 6/8
🧪 Practical for your patients:

1. Consistent sleep-wake times preserve circadian REM architecture
2. Alcohol suppresses REM (even one drink)
3. SSRIs reduce REM latency and increase REM density — monitor in anxiety patients
4. Sleep position matters: supine worsens REM atonia in OSA patients

REM is not passive.
It is the brain's overnight therapy session.

The "sleep on a problem" effect is real:

During REM, the hippocampus fires in "reverse" — replaying sequences at 10-20x speed.

This compression creates novel associations that waking, linear thinking misses.

Tchaikovsky, Kekulé (benzene ring), and Einstein all described insights arriving via sleep.

Your brain runs simulations while you rest.
