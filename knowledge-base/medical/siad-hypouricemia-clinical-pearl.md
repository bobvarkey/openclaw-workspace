# Why is Uric Acid LOW in SIADH?

**Source:** Instagram/medical infographic — Nephrology + Internal Medicine clinical pearl
**Date added:** 2026-03-27

---

## Three-Step Mechanism

### STEP 1 — Water retention in SIADH
- ADH is secreted inappropriately → kidneys retain free water
- → Extracellular fluid (ECF) volume **EXPANDS**

### STEP 2 — Volume expansion affects the kidneys
- ↑ Renal blood flow & GFR → more urate filtered at glomerulus
- ↓ Proximal tubular reabsorption of urate (same as Na⁺)

### STEP 3 — Result: Uricosuria → Hypouricemia
- More urate excreted in urine → serum uric acid drops
- → Low uric acid on labs = clue pointing to SIADH ✓

---

## Clinical Use: SIADH vs CSW

| CONDITION | URIC ACID | VOLUME STATUS |
|---|---|---|
| **SIADH** | ▼ LOW | Volume expanded |
| **CSW** (Cerebral Salt Wasting) | ▲ NORMAL/HIGH | Volume depleted |
| **Hypothyroidism** | ▲ NORMAL/HIGH | ↓ Renal excretion |
| **Adrenal insufficiency** | ▲ NORMAL/HIGH | ↓ Renal excretion |

## Key Takeaway

> "Low uric acid in hyponatremia = strong pointer to SIADH. It's a free lab value that narrows your differential fast!"

---

## Clinical Pearl

In the workup of **hyponatremia**, a **low uric acid** level strongly favors **SIADH** over cerebral salt wasting (CSW) — both can present similarly in neurological patients, but:

- **SIADH:** Volume expanded → increased GFR → uricosuria → hypouricemia
- **CSW:** Volume depleted → decreased GFR → urate retention → normal/high uric acid

This is a useful, cost-effective bedside tool when evaluating hyponatremia in neurocritical care patients.
