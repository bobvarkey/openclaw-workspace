## SCRIPT

### [0–5s] HOOK
> *(Cut to a split screen: brain scan activity on one side, AI neural network animation on the other)*

**Host:** What if I told you AI just learned to *think like a living brain*? Not metaphorically — literally predicting what a mouse's neurons will do next, with 90% accuracy.

---

### [5–20s] CONTEXT — The Breakthrough
> *(Show mouse watching movie clips → brain imaging recording → AI model architecture)*

**Host:** Meet OmniMouse — from Stanford and the University of Göttingen. They trained a massive AI on over **3 million neurons** from 73 mice, across 323 recording sessions.

That's **150 billion neural tokens** — basically, they turned brain activity into a language AI can read. The mice watched natural movies, images, parametric stimuli — and the AI learned to predict their neural firing patterns better than any model ever built.

---

### [20–40s] WHY IT MATTERS — The Inverted Scaling Law
> *(Show a graph: data scaling curve going up vs. model scaling curve flattening)*

**Host:** Here's the wild part. OmniMouse flips the AI scaling story on its head.

With LLMs, you throw more parameters at the problem and performance keeps climbing. But with **brain modeling**, bigger models saturate around 80 million parameters — adding more doesn't help. What *does* help? **More data.** More animals, more stimuli, more behavior tracking. The bottleneck isn't compute — it's that we just don't have enough brain data yet.

This is the first proof that **neural activity follows the same scaling laws as LLMs** — but in reverse.

---

### [40–60s] BIGGER PICTURE — Digital Twins of Brains
> *(Montage: Google synthetic neurons → Allen Institute virtual brain → digital twin concept)*

**Host:** And here's where this gets huge. Combine OmniMouse with what Google just did — **AI-generated synthetic neurons** that speed up brain mapping — and the Allen Institute's petascale **virtual mouse brain simulation**.

We're not just modeling brains anymore. We're building **digital twins of biological intelligence**.

Imagine this: an AI that can simulate what a real mouse brain *would* do in any scenario — without the mouse. In-silico neuroscience. That's the future these papers are building, right now.
