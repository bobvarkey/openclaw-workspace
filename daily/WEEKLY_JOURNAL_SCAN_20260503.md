## JNIS (Journal of NeuroInterventional Surgery)

### 1. DWI-ADC Mismatch Predicts Infarct Growth Rate and EVT Outcomes
**Published:** 16 April 2026

**What it covers:** Diffusion-weighted imaging and ADC mapping to predict infarct growth rate and mechanical thrombectomy outcomes in anterior circulation stroke. DWI-ADC mismatch as imaging biomarker.

**Why it matters clinically:** Current thrombectomy selection relies heavily on time windows or perfusion mismatch. This study suggests DWI-ADC mismatch — which can be assessed rapidly on standard MRI — may be a better predictor of which patients will benefit from thrombectomy and how fast the infarct is growing. If validated, this could change how we image stroke patients before sending them to the angiosuite, especially in late-window cases.

---

### 2. Anesthesia and Thrombectomy: Updated Meta-Analysis
**Published:** February 2026 (JNIS)

**What it covers:** Updated meta-analysis of anesthetic management during endovascular thrombectomy — comparing conscious sedation vs. general anesthesia, and hemodynamic targets during the procedure.

**Why it matters clinically:** There's been ongoing debate about whether GA worsens outcomes in thrombectomy patients due to delays or hemodynamic effects. This updated meta-analysis pulls together more recent trials. Key question: does the anesthetic choice meaningfully affect mRS at 90 days? If you're running a stroke service, this informs your anesthesia protocol. Critical care neurologists managing hemodynamics during thrombectomy should track this.

## INR (Interventional Neuroradiology)

### 3. Flow Diverter Stents with Hydrophilic Polymer Coating for Acutely Ruptured Aneurysms — Single Antiplatelet Therapy
**Published:** 25 April 2026 (OnlineFirst)

**What it covers:** Preliminary experience using flow diverter stents with hydrophilic polymer coating for acutely ruptured aneurysms, with only single antiplatelet therapy (instead of dual antiplatelet).

**Why it matters clinically:** This is a significant practical advance. The main barrier to flow diversion in ruptured aneurysms is the need for dual antiplatelet therapy — particularly problematic when you need to place a stent in an acutely ruptured aneurysm. A hydrophilic polymer coating that allows single antiplatelet coverage could expand the use of flow diverters in the acute rupture setting. Early data only, but promising enough to watch closely.

## AJNR (American Journal of Neuroradiology)

### 4. In-Hospital Stroke vs. Community-Onset Stroke Treated with EVT for LVO
**Published:** April 2026 (Vol 47, Issue 4)

**What it covers:** Comparison of clinical outcomes between in-hospital strokes and community-onset strokes in patients treated with endovascular thrombectomy for large vessel occlusion.

**Why it matters clinically:** In-hospital strokes are a distinct population — they often have different comorbidities, may be post-procedural, and can present diagnostic challenges leading to delays. This study directly addresses whether outcomes from in-hospital EVT are comparable to community-onset. The answer has implications for how aggressively to pursue thrombectomy in stroke patients already admitted to hospital (e.g., post-cardiac surgery, post-intervention). If outcomes are similar, the threshold to intervene should be low.

### 5. Absence of FLAIR Hyperintensity — Therapeutic Window on MRI
**Published:** April 2026 (Vol 47, Issue 4)

**What it covers:** Study showing that absence of FLAIR hyperintensity on MRI suggests the stroke may still be within a therapeutic window — supporting use of MRI to guide treatment decisions.

**Why it matters clinically:** FLAIR imaging has been explored as a surrogate for tissue age in stroke. If no FLAIR hyperintensity is present, even patients beyond traditional time windows may still have salvageable tissue. This supports using MRI — specifically FLAIR — as a decision tool rather than relying solely on time since last known well. For stroke systems where MRI is available, this data helps justify imaging-based decision making over clock-based cutoffs.

## Muscle & Nerve (Clinical Neurophysiology / Neuromuscular)

### 6. Ultrasonographic, Electrophysiological and Clinical Profiles of Motor-Predominant CIDP
**Published:** 2026 (Volume 73, Issue 5)

**What it covers:** Detailed characterization of motor and motor-predominant chronic inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathy (CIDP), using ultrasound imaging combined with electrophysiology and clinical assessment.

**Why it matters clinically:** CIDP has heterogeneous phenotypes. Distinguishing motor-predominant CIDP from other mimics (ALS, multifocal motor neuropathy) is clinically critical but challenging. This study provides data-driven profiles combining nerve ultrasound findings with EMG/NCS — if validated, it could change diagnostic workup for suspected CIDP. Particularly relevant for neurologists ordering EMG and for those evaluating atypical neuropathy presentations.

### 7. Emery-Dreifuss Muscular Dystrophy — Imaging Features and Clinical Correlation
**Published:** Volume 73, Issue 3, 2026

**What it covers:** Imaging features of skeletal muscle in Emery-Dreifuss muscular dystrophy (EMD) and correlation with clinical findings.

**Why it matters clinically:** Emery-Dreifuss is a rare neuromuscular disorder with cardiac implications (arrhythmias, sudden cardiac death). The muscle imaging findings described here — and their correlation with clinical status — could help earlier diagnosis and risk stratification. For neurologists doing muscle imaging or working in neuromuscular clinics, this adds to the genotype-phenotype understanding of EDMD.

## Sleep Medicine

### 8. Daytime Function as Key Outcome Measure for Insomnia Treatment — Real-Time Smartphone Assessment
**Published:** 2026 (University of Maryland / Sleep Medicine)

**What it covers:** New study using real-time smartphone-based assessments to track daytime insomnia symptoms (thinking, fatigue, mood) throughout treatment — rather than relying solely on sleep lab metrics.

**Why it matters clinically:** Insomnia treatments (CBT-I, pharmacotherapy) have traditionally been measured by PSG changes or sleep diaries focused on nighttime sleep. This study challenges that paradigm: daytime function may be a more meaningful endpoint for patients. If daytime cognitive performance and mood improve while nighttime PSG metrics don't fully normalize, the treatment is working. This has direct implications for how you counsel patients on insomnia treatment success and how you design or evaluate outcomes in clinical trials.

---

### 9. Sleep Disturbances and Inflammatory Markers in Depressive and Bipolar Disorders
**Published:** Sleep Medicine, March 2026 (Vol 139)

**What it covers:** Systematic review and meta-analysis of association between sleep disturbances and circulating inflammatory markers (IL-6, CRP, TNF-α) in depressive and bipolar disorders.

**Why it matters clinically:** The link between inflammation and mood disorders is increasingly recognized. Sleep disruption may drive inflammatory processes that worsen mood. This meta-analysis strengthens the evidence base for inflammation as a mediating mechanism. Practically: sleep assessment should be part of psychiatric follow-up, and patients with treatment-resistant depression or bipolar disorder should be screened for sleep disorders. From a neurocritical care angle — ICU delirium and sleep disruption post-ICU are increasingly linked to inflammatory markers too.
