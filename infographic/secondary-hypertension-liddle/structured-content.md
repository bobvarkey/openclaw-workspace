# Diagnosis of Secondary Hypertension — Including Liddle Syndrome

## Learning Objectives
- Identify the major causes of secondary hypertension by category
- Distinguish Liddle Syndrome from other causes using renin-aldosterone profile
- Apply the renin-aldosterone algorithm to guide differential diagnosis
- Recall key treatment differences (amiloride vs spironolactone)

---

## SECTION 1: SECONDARY HYPERTENSION — OVERVIEW
**Key Concept:** Secondary hypertension = identifiable underlying cause; accounts for 5-10% of HTN. Screen young patients (<30y), resistant HTN, or when clues are present.

**Screening Triggers (Visual: checklist icons)**
- Age < 30 with HTN, no risk factors
- Resistant HTN (≥3 drugs including diuretic)
- Accelerated/malignant HTN
- Sudden onset or worsening
- Abdominal bruit, delayed femoral pulses
- Hypokalemia (spontaneous or drug-induced)
- Family history of early HTN or renal disease

---

## SECTION 2: CAUSES BY CATEGORY

### Renal (Visual: kidney icon)
- **Renovascular HTN** (renal artery stenosis)
  - High renin, high aldosterone
  - Unilateral abdominal bruit
- **Renal parenchymal disease** (CKD, GN)
  - Rising Cr, proteinuria, abnormal sediment

### Endocrine (Visual: hormone icon)
- **Primary hyperaldosteronism** (Conn's) — Low renin, HIGH aldosterone. Treat: spironolactone/eplerenone
- **Pheochromocytoma** — Paroxysmal HTN, ↑ metanephrines. Treat: α-blockade → β-blockade → surgery
- **Cushing syndrome** — Cortisol excess, central obesity, striae
- **Thyroid dysfunction** — Hyperthyroid or hypothyroid
- **Hyperparathyroidism** — ↑ calcium

### Vascular (Visual: aorta icon)
- **Coarctation of aorta** — Higher BP in arms > legs, delayed femoral pulses, rib notching on CXR

### Sleep (Visual: sleep icon)
- **OSA** — Daytime somnolence, snoring, obesity, nocturnal hypoxia

### Drug-induced (Visual: pill bottle icon)
- NSAIDs, OCPs, steroids, decongestants, stimulants, licorice, EPO, cyclosporine, tacrolimus

---

## SECTION 3: MONOGENIC (GENETIC) CAUSES — The Rare Ones

### LIDDLE SYNDROME (Pseudo-hyperaldosteronism) ⭐
**(Visual: DNA helix icon — this is the one to emphasize in the broader context)**

**Mechanism:** Autosomal dominant; gain-of-function mutation in ENaC (collecting duct epithelial sodium channel)

**Presentation:**
- Early-onset HTN (childhood/young adult)
- Hypokalemic metabolic alkalosis
- Low renin AND low aldosterone ← KEY distinguishing feature
- No edema
- Family history often present

**Labs:** ↓ K⁺, ↑ HCO₃⁻, ↓ Renin, ↓ Aldosterone, Normal to ↑ Na⁺, Normal AG
**Urine:** High Na⁺, High K⁺
**Imaging:** Usually normal (done to rule out other secondary causes)

**Treatment:**
- ✅ Amiloride or triamterene (direct ENaC blockers)
- ❌ Spironolactone NOT effective (blocks aldosterone receptor; defect is downstream)

### Other Monogenic HTN
- **Gordon syndrome** — Hyperkalemic HTN, WNK mutations
- **Apparent mineralocorticoid excess (AME)** — 11β-HSD2 deficiency; licorice toxicity mimics this
- **Glucocorticoid-remediable aldosteronism** — Aldosterone high; responds to dexamethasone
- **CAH** (11β-OH / 17α-OH deficiency)

---

## SECTION 4: THE RENIN-ALDOSTERONE ALGORITHM ⭐
**(Visual: decision tree / flow diagram)**

| Renin | Aldosterone | Diagnosis |
|-------|-------------|-----------|
| ↑ High | ↑ High | Renovascular HTN, malignant HTN, reninoma |
| ↓ Low | ↑ HIGH | **Primary hyperaldosteronism (Conn's)** |
| ↓ Low | ↓ LOW | **Liddle syndrome**, Gordon syndrome, AME, licorice, CAH, Cushing |
| Normal | Normal | Essential HTN (diagnosis of exclusion), renal parenchymal disease |

**Clinical Pearl:** Liddle is the only cause where BOTH renin AND aldosterone are LOW despite HTN and hypokalemia. This is the hallmark.

---

## SECTION 5: TREATMENT PEARLS
**(Visual: pill icons for each)**

| Condition | Treatment |
|-----------|-----------|
| Liddle Syndrome | **Amiloride / Triamterene** (ENaC blockers) |
| Conn's | **Spironolactone / Eplerenone** (aldosterone receptor antagonists) |
| Renovascular HTN | Revascularization + ACEi/ARB |
| Pheochromocytoma | α-blockade (phenoxybenzamine) → β-blockade → surgery |
| OSA | CPAP, weight loss |
| Coarctation | Surgical repair or balloon angioplasty |
