# Diagnosis of Secondary Hypertension — Including Liddle Syndrome

## Overview
Secondary hypertension accounts for 5-10% of all hypertension cases. Identifying the underlying cause is critical as it may be curable and guides targeted treatment. Liddle Syndrome is a rare monogenic cause that mimics hyperaldosteronism but with suppressed renin AND aldosterone.

## Common Causes of Secondary Hypertension

### Renal Causes
- Renovascular hypertension (renal artery stenosis)
  - High renin, high aldosterone
  - Unilateral bruit on exam
- Renal parenchymal disease (CKD, glomerulonephritis)
  - Rising creatinine, proteinuria, abnormal urine sediment

### Endocrine Causes
- Primary hyperaldosteronism (Conn's syndrome)
  - Low renin, HIGH aldosterone
  - Treat with spironolactone/eplerenone
- Pheochromocytoma
  - Paroxysmal hypertension, headache, palpitations, diaphoresis
  - High metanephrines on plasma/urine
- Cushing syndrome
  - Cortisol excess, central obesity, striae, easy bruising
- Thyroid disorders (hyperthyroidism or hypothyroidism)
- Hyperparathyroidism (↑ calcium)

### Vascular Causes
- Coarctation of aorta
  - Higher BP in arms than legs
  - Delayed femoral pulses, rib notching on CXR
  - Usually congenital, presents young

### Sleep-Related Causes
- Obstructive sleep apnea
  - Daytime somnolence, snoring, obesity
  - Nocturnal hypoxia, high sympathetic tone

### Drug-induced / Exogenous
- NSAIDs, oral contraceptives, steroids, decongestants, stimulants, licorice, EPO, cyclosporine, tacrolimus

## Monogenic (Genetic) Causes — The Rare Ones

### Liddle Syndrome (Pseudo-hyperaldosteronism)
- **Mechanism:** Autosomal dominant, gain-of-function mutation in ENaC (epithelial sodium channel) in the collecting duct
- **Presentation:** Early-onset hypertension (often childhood/young adult); hypokalemic metabolic alkalosis; suppressed renin AND aldosterone
- **Key differentiating feature:** Low renin AND low aldosterone (unlike Conn's where aldosterone is high)
- **Exam findings:** No edema
- **Labs:** ↓ K⁺, ↑ HCO₃⁻, ↓ renin, ↓ aldosterone, normal to ↑ Na⁺, normal anion gap
- **Urine:** High Na⁺, high K⁺
- **Imaging:** Usually normal (done to rule out secondary causes)
- **Treatment:** ENaC blockers — amiloride or triamterene
- **NOT effective:** Spironolactone (blocks aldosterone receptor, but defect is downstream)
- **Family history:** Often present

### Other Monogenic HTN
- Gordon syndrome (pseudohypoaldosteronism type II) — hyperkalemic HTN, WNK mutations
- Apparent mineralocorticoid excess (AME) — 11β-HSD2 deficiency, licorice toxicity
- Glucocorticoid-remediable aldosteronism (GRA) — aldosterone high, responds to dexamethasone
- Congenital adrenal hyperplasia (11β-hydroxylase / 17α-hydroxylase deficiency)

## Diagnostic Approach to Secondary Hypertension

### Screening Triggers
- Age < 30 with HTN without risk factors
- Resistant HTN (≥3 drugs including diuretic)
- Accelerated/malignant HTN
- Sudden onset or worsening of HTN
- Abdominal bruit, delayed femoral pulses
- Hypokalemia (spontaneous or diuretic-induced)
- Family history of early-onset HTN or renal disease

### Initial Workup
- Serum electrolytes (K⁺, HCO₃⁻)
- Creatinine/eGFR, urinalysis
- Plasma renin activity (PRA) and aldosterone concentration
- Overnight 1 mg dexamethasone suppression test (if Cushing suspected)
- Plasma metanephrines / 24-hour urine for catecholamines
- Renal artery duplex / CT angiography (if renovascular HTN suspected)
- Polysomnography (if OSA suspected)

### The Renin-Aldosterone Algorithm
- **High renin, high aldosterone** → Renovascular HTN, malignant HTN, reninoma
- **Low renin, high aldosterone** → Primary hyperaldosteronism (Conn's)
- **Low renin, low aldosterone** → Liddle syndrome, Gordon syndrome, AME, licorice, CAH, Cushing
- **Normal renin, normal aldosterone** → Essential HTN (diagnosis of exclusion), renal parenchymal disease

## Treatment Pearls
- Cause-specific treatment is key
- For Liddle: amiloride/triamterene (direct ENaC blockers)
- For Conn's: spironolactone/eplerenone (aldosterone receptor antagonists)
- For renovascular: revascularization + ACEi/ARB
- For pheochromocytoma: α-blockade first (phenoxybenzamine), then β-blockade, then surgical resection
